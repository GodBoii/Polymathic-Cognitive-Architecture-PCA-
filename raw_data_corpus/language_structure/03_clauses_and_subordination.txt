A TREATISE ON CLAUSES AND SUBORDINATION: THE HIERARCHY OF THOUGHT IN LANGUAGE

CHAPTER I: ON THE NATURE OF CLAUSES

A clause, that fundamental unit of syntactic structure, is a group of words containing a subject and a predicate. Unlike the phrase, which lacks either subject or predicate or both, the clause possesses both essential elements of sentence structure. The clause thus represents a complete thought, though it may not stand alone as a complete sentence.

Clauses are of two principal kinds: the independent clause, which can stand alone as a sentence; and the dependent clause, which cannot stand alone but must function within a larger construction. It is through the combination of independent and dependent clauses that language achieves the expression of complex relationships among ideas—the coordination of equals, the subordination of one thought to another, the embedding of concepts within concepts.

CHAPTER II: ON THE INDEPENDENT CLAUSE

The independent clause, also termed the principal clause or main clause, expresses a complete thought and can stand alone as a sentence. It contains a subject and a predicate, and it requires no other clause to complete its meaning.

THE SIMPLE INDEPENDENT CLAUSE

The simple independent clause contains one subject and one predicate, each of which may be modified but neither of which is compounded:

"The scholar studied the ancient text."
"Her patient investigation revealed the truth."
"The manuscript, discovered in the archive, contained valuable information."

THE COMPOUND SUBJECT OR PREDICATE

The independent clause may contain a compound subject—two or more subjects sharing the same predicate—or a compound predicate—two or more predicates sharing the same subject:

"The scholar and her assistant studied the ancient text." (Compound subject)
"The scholar studied the ancient text and discovered its significance." (Compound predicate)
"The scholar and her assistant studied the ancient text and discovered its significance." (Both compound)

These compound elements do not create multiple clauses; rather, they create single clauses with multiple subjects or multiple predicates.

MULTIPLE INDEPENDENT CLAUSES

When a sentence contains more than one independent clause, the clauses may be joined by coordination, forming a compound sentence:

"The scholar studied the ancient text, and she discovered its significance."

Here, two independent clauses—"The scholar studied the ancient text" and "she discovered its significance"—are joined by the coordinating conjunction "and." Each could stand alone as a sentence; together, they form a compound sentence.

CHAPTER III: ON THE DEPENDENT CLAUSE

The dependent clause, also termed the subordinate clause, cannot stand alone as a sentence. It contains a subject and a predicate, yet it functions within a larger construction—as a noun, an adjective, or an adverb. The dependent clause is introduced by a subordinating word: a subordinating conjunction, a relative pronoun, or a relative adverb.

DEPENDENT CLAUSES AS NOUNS

A noun clause functions as a noun within the sentence. It may serve as subject, object, subject complement, or object of a preposition.

As subject: "What she discovered surprised everyone."
As direct object: "I know that she is innocent."
As subject complement: "The question is whether we should proceed."
As object of a preposition: "She listened to what he advised."

The noun clause is introduced by a subordinating conjunction ("that," "whether," "if") or by an interrogative word serving as a subordinator ("who," "what," "where," "when," "why," "how").

DEPENDENT CLAUSES AS ADJECTIVES

An adjective clause, also termed a relative clause, functions as an adjective, modifying a noun or pronoun. It is introduced by a relative pronoun ("who," "whom," "whose," "which," "that") or by a relative adverb ("where," "when," "why").

"The scholar who discovered the text was renowned." (Modifies "scholar")
"The text which she discovered was ancient." (Modifies "text")
"The archive where the text was found is famous." (Modifies "archive")
"The time when the discovery was made was significant." (Modifies "time")
"The reason why she studied the text remains unclear." (Modifies "reason")

Adjective clauses may be restrictive or nonrestrictive. A restrictive clause limits or defines the noun it modifies; it provides information essential to identification. A nonrestrictive clause adds information about the noun but does not limit it; it is set off by commas.

Restrictive: "The scholar who discovered the text was renowned." (Specifies which scholar)
Nonrestrictive: "The scholar, who discovered the text, was renowned." (Adds information about the scholar)

DEPENDENT CLAUSES AS ADVERBS

An adverb clause functions as an adverb, modifying a verb, adjective, or adverb. It is introduced by a subordinating conjunction that indicates the semantic relationship: time, place, manner, cause, purpose, condition, concession, or comparison.

Time: "When she arrived, the meeting had ended."
Place: "Where the river bends, there is a deep pool."
Manner: "He spoke as if he were an expert."
Cause: "Because she was tired, she retired early."
Purpose: "She studied hard so that she might succeed."
Condition: "If you consent, I will proceed."
Concession: "Although he was wealthy, he lived simply."
Comparison: "She is taller than her sister is."

The adverb clause may precede or follow the independent clause it modifies. When it precedes, it is typically set off by a comma; when it follows, the comma is often omitted unless the clause is parenthetical.

CHAPTER IV: ON THE SUBORDINATING CONJUNCTIONS

Subordinating conjunctions introduce dependent clauses and establish semantic relationships between the dependent clause and the independent clause. These conjunctions may be classified according to the relationships they indicate.

CONJUNCTIONS OF TIME

These conjunctions indicate temporal relationships: "when," "whenever," "while," "before," "after," "until," "till," "since," "as soon as," "as long as," "once."

"When the clock struck midnight, the transformation occurred."
"She had departed before the letter arrived."
"Remain here until I return."
"He has been melancholy since she left."

CONJUNCTIONS OF PLACE

These conjunctions indicate spatial relationships: "where," "wherever," "whence."

"Where the river meets the sea, there is a harbor."
"Wherever she went, she found friends."
"Return whence you came."

CONJUNCTIONS OF MANNER

These conjunctions indicate the way in which something is done: "as," "as if," "as though," "how."

"He spoke as one who has authority."
"She looked as if she had seen a ghost."
"Do as you have been taught."
"He wondered how she had accomplished it."

CONJUNCTIONS OF CAUSE

These conjunctions indicate cause or reason: "because," "since," "as," "for," "inasmuch as."

"Because she was weary, she rested."
"Since you have asked, I will explain."
"As it was raining, we remained indoors."
"He was content, for his work was done."

CONJUNCTIONS OF PURPOSE

These conjunctions indicate purpose or intended result: "that," "so that," "in order that," "lest."

"She studied that she might pass."
"He spoke loudly so that all could hear."
"They departed early in order that they might arrive before nightfall."
"He feared lest he should be discovered."

CONJUNCTIONS OF CONDITION

These conjunctions indicate conditions under which something occurs: "if," "unless," "provided that," "providing that," "supposing that," "suppose," "in case," "on condition that."

"If you study, you will succeed."
"Unless you hurry, you will be late."
"Provided that the weather is fair, we shall proceed."
"In case of fire, break the glass."

CONJUNCTIONS OF CONCESSION

These conjunctions indicate that something is true despite something else: "although," "though," "even though," "even if," "while," "whereas."

"Although she was tired, she continued working."
"Though the task was difficult, it was accomplished."
"Even if you refuse, I shall proceed."
"While I agree with your conclusion, I question your premises."

CONJUNCTIONS OF COMPARISON

These conjunctions indicate comparison: "than," "as."

"She is wiser than her sister is."
"He is not so tall as his brother."

CHAPTER V: ON THE RELATIVE PRONOUNS

Relative pronouns introduce adjective clauses and connect them to their antecedents—the nouns or pronouns they modify. The relative pronouns are "who," "whom," "whose," "which," and "that."

WHO, WHOM, WHOSE

"Who" is used for persons, in the nominative case (as subject of the clause). "Whom" is used for persons, in the objective case (as object of the verb or preposition). "Whose" is used for persons (and sometimes for things), in the possessive case.

"The scholar who wrote the text was famous." (Subject of "wrote")
"The scholar whom we met was famous." (Object of "met")
"The scholar whose work we admired was famous." (Possessive)

WHICH

"Which" is used for things and animals, in either the nominative or objective case.

"The text which she discovered was ancient." (Nominative, subject of "discovered")
"The text which she studied was ancient." (Objective, object of "studied")

THAT

"That" is used for persons, things, and animals, in either the nominative or objective case. It is preferred in restrictive clauses.

"The scholar that wrote the text was famous."
"The text that she discovered was ancient."

The relative pronoun takes its case from its function within the dependent clause, not from its antecedent. Thus, in "The scholar whom we met," "whom" is objective because it is the object of "met," even though "scholar" would be nominative if it were the subject of the independent clause.

OMISSION OF THE RELATIVE PRONOUN

When the relative pronoun is the object of the verb in the dependent clause, it may be omitted:

"The text she discovered was ancient." (Omitted "which" or "that")
"The scholar we met was famous." (Omitted "whom" or "that")

When the relative pronoun is the subject of the dependent clause, it cannot be omitted:

"The scholar who wrote the text was famous." (Cannot omit "who")

CHAPTER VI: ON THE RELATIVE ADVERBS

Relative adverbs introduce adjective clauses and connect them to nouns expressing time, place, or reason. The relative adverbs are "where," "when," and "why."

WHERE

"Where" introduces clauses modifying nouns of place:

"The house where she was born still stands."
"The city where they met is famous."

WHEN

"When" introduces clauses modifying nouns of time:

"The day when she arrived was memorable."
"The era when the empire flourished is remembered."

WHY

"Why" introduces clauses modifying nouns of reason:

"The reason why she departed remains unknown."
"The cause why he failed is clear."

CHAPTER VII: ON NESTED AND EMBEDDED CLAUSES

Clauses may be nested within clauses, creating complex hierarchical structures. This embedding enables the expression of intricate relationships among ideas.

SIMPLE EMBEDDING

A dependent clause may contain another dependent clause:

"She said that she believed that he was innocent."

Here, the independent clause is "She said." The first dependent clause, "that she believed that he was innocent," functions as the object of "said." Within this clause, another dependent clause, "that he was innocent," functions as the object of "believed."

MULTIPLE EMBEDDING

Multiple clauses may be embedded at different levels:

"The scholar who discovered the text that had been lost for centuries was celebrated by the university where she had studied."

Analysis:
- Independent clause: "The scholar was celebrated by the university"
- First adjective clause: "who discovered the text" (modifies "scholar")
- Second adjective clause: "that had been lost for centuries" (modifies "text")
- Third adjective clause: "where she had studied" (modifies "university")

Such embedding must be carefully controlled, lest the sentence become unwieldy and obscure. The reader must be able to trace the relationships among the clauses; excessive embedding may overwhelm the reader's capacity to hold the structure in mind.

CHAPTER VIII: ON THE SEQUENCE OF TENSES IN DEPENDENT CLAUSES

The tense of the verb in a dependent clause is governed by the tense of the verb in the independent clause, according to the principle of the sequence of tenses.

PRIMARY SEQUENCE

When the principal verb is in a present, future, or present perfect tense, the subordinate verb may be in any tense appropriate to the meaning:

"She says that she is tired." (Present in both)
"She says that she was tired." (Present principal, past subordinate)
"She says that she will be tired." (Present principal, future subordinate)

SECONDARY SEQUENCE

When the principal verb is in a past tense, the subordinate verb must also be in a past tense:

"She said that she was tired." (Past in both)
"She said that she had been tired." (Past principal, past perfect subordinate)
"She said that she would be tired." (Past principal, future-in-the-past subordinate)

EXCEPTIONS

The sequence of tenses may be violated when the subordinate clause expresses a general truth:

"She said that the earth is round." (Not "was round," because the earth's roundness is a permanent fact)

The sequence may also be violated when the subordinate clause expresses something still true or still future at the time of speaking:

"She said that she will arrive tomorrow." (If tomorrow has not yet come)

Thus through the mechanism of subordination does language achieve the hierarchical organization of thought, the expression of relationships among ideas, the embedding of concepts within concepts. The mastery of clauses and subordination marks the capacity for complex expression, for the construction of discourse that mirrors the complexity of thought itself.
