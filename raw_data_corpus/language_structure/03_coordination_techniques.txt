THE ART OF COORDINATION: A TREATISE ON CONNECTING EQUAL ELEMENTS IN DISCOURSE

CHAPTER I: ON THE NATURE OF COORDINATION

Coordination, that fundamental operation whereby elements of equal grammatical rank are joined together, stands as one of the two principal methods of combining linguistic units into larger structures. Unlike subordination, which creates hierarchical relationships between elements, coordination creates relationships of equality, linking together words, phrases, or clauses that stand upon the same level of grammatical importance. Through coordination, the simple utterance expands into the compound, the isolated idea joins with its fellows, and discourse achieves both breadth and balance.

The coordinating conjunctions—those humble yet indispensable words that effect the connection—are seven in number: "and," "but," "or," "nor," "for," "yet," "so." Each carries its particular semantic force, joining elements while simultaneously indicating the nature of the relationship between them. Beyond these, certain conjunctive adverbs and punctuation marks—the semicolon, the colon—may also serve coordinative functions, creating connections that are at once more subtle and more various.

CHAPTER II: ON THE COORDINATING CONJUNCTIONS

THE CONJUNCTION "AND"

The conjunction "and" indicates addition, continuation, or consequence. It joins elements that are to be considered together, whether simply accumulated or causally connected.

Addition: "The scholar studied the manuscript, and the assistant catalogued the artifacts."
Here, two independent actions are joined, each complementing the other in the overall description of the research endeavor.

Continuation: "She opened the door and entered the chamber."
The two actions are presented as sequential, one following naturally upon the other.

Consequence: "He studied diligently, and he passed the examination."
Here, the second clause presents a result of the first; the "and" carries causal force.

The conjunction "and" may also join nouns, adjectives, verbs, adverbs, prepositional phrases, and subordinate clauses:

Nouns: "The scholar and the assistant examined the manuscript."
Adjectives: "The ancient and valuable manuscript was carefully preserved."
Verbs: "She studied and wrote until dawn."
Adverbs: "She worked carefully and methodically."
Prepositional phrases: "The book lay on the table and under the lamp."
Subordinate clauses: "She said that she would come and that she would bring the documents."

THE CONJUNCTION "BUT"

The conjunction "but" indicates contrast, exception, or opposition. It joins elements that stand in some oppositional relationship, each qualifying or limiting the other.

Contrast: "The manuscript was ancient, but it was remarkably well preserved."
The second clause contrasts with what might be expected from the first.

Exception: "She studied diligently, but she failed the examination."
The second clause presents an exception to what the first clause might lead one to expect.

Opposition: "He wanted to proceed, but his conscience forbade it."
The two clauses present opposed inclinations or forces.

The conjunction "but" may join elements of various types:

Nouns: "Not gold but silver was used in the construction."
Adjectives: "The solution was simple but effective."
Verbs: "She hoped but did not expect success."
Clauses: "The evidence was compelling, but the jury remained unconvinced."

THE CONJUNCTION "OR"

The conjunction "or" indicates alternative, choice, or possibility. It presents elements as options, one of which may be selected or may prove true.

Alternative: "She could accept the offer or decline it."
The two options are presented as mutually exclusive possibilities.

Possibility: "The author may have been a monk or a merchant."
The two possibilities are presented as alternatives, one of which may be true.

Condition: "Improve your work or face dismissal."
The second clause presents the consequence of not choosing the first.

The conjunction "or" may join elements of various types:

Nouns: "Tea or coffee will be served."
Adjectives: "The solution was elegant or practical, depending on one's perspective."
Verbs: "She could stay or leave as she chose."
Clauses: "She must consent, or the project cannot proceed."

THE CONJUNCTION "NOR"

The conjunction "nor" indicates negative addition. It joins a negative element to another negative, emphasizing the extension of negation.

"She would not consent to the demand, nor would she refuse it outright."
"He had neither the time nor the inclination to pursue the matter."
"The evidence was not compelling, nor was it entirely absent."

The conjunction "nor" typically follows a negative clause and introduces another negative clause, often with inversion of subject and auxiliary verb. It may also appear in the construction "neither...nor," joining two negative elements.

THE CONJUNCTION "FOR"

The conjunction "for" indicates cause or reason. It introduces a clause that provides the reason for the statement made in the preceding clause.

"She was exhausted, for she had worked through the night."
"He declined the invitation, for he had other obligations."
"The manuscript was valuable, for it contained the only surviving copy of the text."

The conjunction "for" differs from "because" in that it introduces the reason as an afterthought or explanation, rather than as the primary focus of the sentence. The clause introduced by "for" typically follows the independent clause and is set off by a comma.

THE CONJUNCTION "YET"

The conjunction "yet" indicates contrast with an element of surprise or unexpectedness. It is similar to "but" but carries a stronger sense of something contrary to expectation.

"The task was difficult, yet they completed it successfully."
"He was exhausted, yet he continued working."
"The evidence was scanty, yet the conclusion seemed certain."

The conjunction "yet" may also appear in the construction "and yet," which combines addition with contrast:

"The evidence was scanty, and yet the conclusion seemed certain."

THE CONJUNCTION "SO"

The conjunction "so" indicates result or consequence. It introduces a clause that presents the outcome or effect of the situation described in the preceding clause.

"She was tired, so she retired early."
"He studied diligently, so he passed the examination."
"The weather was inclement, so the ceremony was postponed."

The conjunction "so" differs from "therefore" and "consequently" in its more colloquial tone. In formal writing, "therefore" or "consequently" may be preferred.

CHAPTER III: ON CORRELATIVE CONJUNCTIONS

Correlative conjunctions are pairs of words that work together to join coordinate elements. They create a structure in which the joined elements are presented in parallel form, emphasizing their equality and connection.

"BOTH...AND"

The correlative "both...and" joins two elements, emphasizing their joint presence or occurrence:

"She was both intelligent and industrious."
"The manuscript was both ancient and valuable."
"Both the scholar and the assistant contributed to the discovery."

The joined elements must be parallel in structure: both nouns, both adjectives, both clauses, etc.

"EITHER...OR"

The correlative "either...or" presents two alternatives, emphasizing that one must be chosen or one must be true:

"Either she will accept the offer, or she will decline it."
"The author was either a monk or a merchant."
"Either the manuscript is genuine, or it is a clever forgery."

"NEITHER...NOR"

The correlative "neither...nor" joins two negative elements, emphasizing the negation of both:

"She was neither wealthy nor famous."
"The manuscript was neither ancient nor valuable."
"Neither the scholar nor the assistant could decipher the text."

"NOT ONLY...BUT ALSO"

The correlative "not only...but also" joins two elements, emphasizing that both are present and that the second is perhaps more significant or surprising:

"She was not only intelligent but also wise."
"The manuscript was not only ancient but also remarkably well preserved."
"He not only discovered the text but also translated it."

"NOT...BUT"

The correlative "not...but" joins two elements, emphasizing the negation of the first and the affirmation of the second:

"The material was not gold but silver."
"She was not discouraged but encouraged by the criticism."
"The solution was not complex but simple."

"WHETHER...OR"

The correlative "whether...or" presents alternatives, often in contexts of uncertainty or indifference:

"Whether she accepts or declines, the outcome will be significant."
"I do not know whether the manuscript is genuine or forged."
"Whether by skill or by fortune, he succeeded in his endeavor."

CHAPTER IV: ON THE SEMICOLON AS A COORDINATING DEVICE

The semicolon, that elegant mark of punctuation, serves to join independent clauses without the use of a coordinating conjunction. The connection between the clauses is thus left to be inferred from their content, creating a relationship that is often more subtle and more various than that indicated by the conjunctions.

THE SEMICOLON ALONE

When the semicolon joins independent clauses without a conjunction, the relationship must be inferred:

"The manuscript was ancient; its pages were yellowed with age."
"She studied diligently; she passed the examination."
"He declined the offer; his conscience would not permit acceptance."

In the first example, the relationship is one of elaboration; the second clause expands upon the first. In the second, the relationship is causal; the second clause presents a result. In the third, the relationship is explanatory; the second clause provides the reason for the first.

THE SEMICOLON WITH CONJUNCTIVE ADVERB

The semicolon may be followed by a conjunctive adverb, which clarifies the relationship between the clauses:

"The manuscript was ancient; moreover, it was remarkably well preserved."
"She studied diligently; consequently, she passed the examination."
"He declined the offer; nevertheless, he remained on friendly terms."

The conjunctive adverbs include:
- Addition: "moreover," "furthermore," "besides," "in addition"
- Contrast: "however," "nevertheless," "nonetheless," "still," "yet"
- Result: "therefore," "consequently," "accordingly," "thus," "hence"
- Time: "meanwhile," "then," "subsequently," "thereafter"
- Exemplification: "for example," "for instance"
- Clarification: "namely," "that is," "in other words"

THE SEMICOLON IN A SERIES

When items in a series contain internal punctuation, the semicolon separates the items to avoid confusion:

"The committee included three scholars: Professor A, the chairman; Professor B, the secretary; and Professor C, the treasurer."
"She visited three cities: Paris, France; London, England; and Rome, Italy."

CHAPTER V: ON THE COLON AS A COORDINATING DEVICE

The colon, though primarily used to introduce elements that follow upon a statement, may serve a coordinative function in joining clauses. The colon indicates that what follows explains, illustrates, or fulfills what precedes.

THE COLON INTRODUCING AN EXPLANATION

"The reason was clear: the manuscript had been misattributed."
"She faced a dilemma: she could not both tell the truth and spare his feelings."
"The conclusion was inescapable: the artifact was a forgery."

THE COLON INTRODUCING A LIST

"Three possibilities remained: the manuscript was genuine, it was a forgery, or it was a later copy."
"She required several items: paper, ink, quills, and sealing wax."

THE COLON INTRODUCING A QUOTATION

"The scholar declared: 'This manuscript changes everything we thought we knew.'"
"She wrote in her journal: 'The discovery has exceeded all expectations.'"

CHAPTER VI: ON PARALLELISM IN COORDINATION

Coordination requires parallelism: the joined elements must be of the same grammatical form. This principle, though fundamental, is often violated in careless writing.

PARALLEL NOUNS

Correct: "She studied the manuscript and the artifact."
Incorrect: "She studied the manuscript and examining the artifact."

PARALLEL ADJECTIVES

Correct: "The manuscript was ancient and valuable."
Incorrect: "The manuscript was ancient and of great value."

PARALLEL VERBS

Correct: "She studied the text and translated it."
Incorrect: "She studied the text and the translation of it."

PARALLEL CLAUSES

Correct: "She knew that the manuscript was valuable and that it should be preserved."
Incorrect: "She knew that the manuscript was valuable and of the need to preserve it."

The requirement for parallelism extends to correlatives:

Correct: "She was both intelligent and industrious."
Incorrect: "She was both intelligent and an industrious worker."

Correct: "The manuscript was not only ancient but also valuable."
Incorrect: "The manuscript was not only ancient but also of great value."

CHAPTER VII: ON THE RHETORIC OF COORDINATION

Coordination serves not merely grammatical but rhetorical purposes. The choice to coordinate rather than subordinate reflects a choice about how ideas should be presented and related.

COORDINATION FOR EQUALITY

When ideas are of equal importance, coordination presents them as such:

"The scholar discovered the manuscript, and the assistant preserved it."

Both actions are presented as equally significant. Had the author wished to emphasize one over the other, subordination would have been appropriate:

"The scholar discovered the manuscript, which the assistant preserved."

COORDINATION FOR CUMULATIVE EFFECT

Coordination may create a cumulative effect, building intensity through the accumulation of parallel elements:

"She studied. She researched. She analyzed. She wrote. She revised. She published."

The series of short, coordinated sentences creates a sense of relentless effort.

COORDINATION FOR BALANCE

Coordination may create balance, presenting ideas in symmetrical form:

"To err is human; to forgive, divine."
"Ask not what your country can do for you; ask what you can do for your country."

The balanced structure creates rhetorical force and memorability.

COORDINATION FOR CONTRAST

Coordination with "but" or "yet" creates contrast, emphasizing the difference between the joined elements:

"The task was simple, yet the consequences were profound."
"He spoke softly, but his words carried great weight."

Thus through coordination does language achieve the combination of equals, the accumulation of like elements, the balance of opposed ideas, and the connection of independent thoughts. The mastery of coordination marks the capacity for discourse that is at once various and coherent, complex and clear.
