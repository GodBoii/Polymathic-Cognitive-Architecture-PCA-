THE ART OF SUBORDINATION: A TREATISE ON HIERARCHICAL STRUCTURES IN DISCOURSE

CHAPTER I: ON THE NATURE OF SUBORDINATION

Subordination, that sophisticated operation whereby one element is made dependent upon another, stands as the principal method whereby language achieves hierarchical organization. Unlike coordination, which joins elements of equal rank, subordination creates relationships of dependence, embedding one idea within another, making one clause serve the purposes of another. Through subordination, the simple utterance expands into the complex, the isolated thought finds its place within a larger structure, and discourse achieves the depth and intricacy that characterize mature expression.

The subordinate clause, introduced by a subordinating word, cannot stand alone as a sentence. It functions within the larger sentence as a noun, an adjective, or an adverb, contributing to the meaning of the main clause without asserting independent existence. The choice to subordinate rather than coordinate reflects a judgment about the relative importance of ideas, about which should be foregrounded and which should provide support, context, or qualification.

CHAPTER II: SUBORDINATION BY MEANS OF NOUN CLAUSES

The noun clause, functioning as a noun within the sentence, enables the expression of complex propositional content in positions where a simple noun might otherwise appear. Through the noun clause, entire propositions—complete thoughts with their own subjects and predicates—serve as subjects, objects, or complements within larger constructions.

NOUN CLAUSES AS SUBJECTS

The noun clause may serve as the subject of a sentence, the entire proposition functioning as that about which something is asserted:

"What she discovered astonished the scholarly community."
"That the manuscript was authentic soon became apparent."
"Whether the attribution was correct remained uncertain."
"How she accomplished the translation puzzled her colleagues."

In each case, the noun clause occupies the position of the subject, and the verb of the main clause expresses what is asserted about that subject. The construction enables the discussion of propositions as entities, making thoughts themselves the objects of thought.

NOUN CLAUSES AS OBJECTS

The noun clause may serve as the direct object of a transitive verb, the proposition functioning as that which is perceived, known, believed, or otherwise acted upon:

"She knew that the manuscript was valuable."
"He discovered what had been hidden for centuries."
"They understood why the author had remained anonymous."
"She realized how significant the discovery was."

Verbs that commonly take noun clause objects include verbs of cognition ("know," "believe," "understand," "realize"), verbs of perception ("see," "hear," "notice"), verbs of communication ("say," "report," "claim"), and verbs of decision ("decide," "determine," "resolve").

NOUN CLAUSES AS SUBJECT COMPLEMENTS

The noun clause may serve as a subject complement, following a linking verb and equating the subject with the propositional content:

"The question is whether the manuscript is authentic."
"The problem was that no copies survived."
"The mystery remains who wrote the text."
"The concern was how the discovery would be received."

NOUN CLAUSES AS OBJECTS OF PREPOSITIONS

The noun clause may serve as the object of a preposition, the proposition functioning as the object of the prepositional relationship:

"She thought about what she had discovered."
"He wondered about where the manuscript had originated."
"They argued about whether the text was genuine."
"She reflected on how the discovery had changed her life."

INTRODUCTORY WORDS FOR NOUN CLAUSES

Noun clauses are introduced by various words, each carrying its particular semantic contribution:

"That" introduces noun clauses stating facts, without adding semantic content of its own:
"She knew that the manuscript was valuable."

"Whether" and "if" introduce noun clauses expressing alternatives or uncertainty:
"She wondered whether the manuscript was valuable."
"She asked if the manuscript was valuable."

Interrogative words—"who," "what," "which," "where," "when," "why," "how"—introduce noun clauses that express questions indirectly:
"She discovered what was hidden."
"She knew who had written the text."
"She understood why the author had remained anonymous."

CHAPTER III: SUBORDINATION BY MEANS OF ADJECTIVE CLAUSES

The adjective clause, also termed the relative clause, modifies a noun or pronoun, providing information about the entity designated by that noun or pronoun. Through the adjective clause, the simple designation is enriched with description, identification, or qualification.

RESTRICTIVE ADJECTIVE CLAUSES

The restrictive adjective clause limits or defines the noun it modifies, providing information essential to identifying the entity designated:

"The scholar who discovered the manuscript was renowned."
"The manuscript which she found was ancient."
"The archive where the document was stored is famous."
"The time when the discovery was made was significant."

In each case, the adjective clause specifies which scholar, which manuscript, which archive, which time. Without the clause, the identity would be unclear. Restrictive clauses are not set off by commas.

NONRESTRICTIVE ADJECTIVE CLAUSES

The nonrestrictive adjective clause adds information about the noun without limiting or defining it, the identity being already clear from context:

"The scholar, who had spent decades in research, was finally recognized."
"The manuscript, which had been lost for centuries, was remarkably preserved."
"The archive, where countless treasures were stored, attracted scholars worldwide."
"The year 1847, when the discovery was made, marked a turning point."

In each case, the identity is clear without the clause; the clause adds supplementary information. Nonrestrictive clauses are set off by commas.

RELATIVE PRONOUNS AND THEIR USES

"Who" is used for persons, in the nominative case, as subject of the clause:
"The scholar who wrote the text was famous."

"Whom" is used for persons, in the objective case, as object of the verb or preposition:
"The scholar whom we met was famous."
"The scholar to whom the letter was addressed was famous."

"Whose" is used for persons (and sometimes things), in the possessive case:
"The scholar whose work we admired was famous."
"The manuscript whose author is unknown remains a mystery."

"Which" is used for things, in either nominative or objective case:
"The manuscript which she discovered was ancient."
"The manuscript which was discovered was ancient."

"That" is used for persons or things, primarily in restrictive clauses:
"The scholar that wrote the text was famous."
"The manuscript that she discovered was ancient."

REDUCED ADJECTIVE CLAUSES

Adjective clauses may be reduced to phrases, creating more concise constructions:

Reduced to participial phrase:
"The scholar studying the manuscript made a discovery." (From "who was studying")
"The manuscript discovered in the archive was ancient." (From "which was discovered")

Reduced to prepositional phrase:
"The manuscript on the table was ancient." (From "which was on the table")

CHAPTER IV: SUBORDINATION BY MEANS OF ADVERB CLAUSES

The adverb clause modifies a verb, adjective, or adverb, expressing the circumstances under which the main clause action occurs—time, place, manner, cause, purpose, condition, concession, or comparison. Through the adverb clause, the main assertion is situated within a context that enriches its meaning.

ADVERB CLAUSES OF TIME

Adverb clauses of time indicate when the main clause action occurs, introduced by conjunctions such as "when," "whenever," "while," "before," "after," "until," "since," "as soon as":

"When she arrived, the meeting had already begun."
"Whenever she visited, she brought gifts."
"While she studied, he worked."
"Before she departed, she said goodbye."
"After she finished, she rested."
"She worked until she was exhausted."
"She has been studying since she arrived."
"As soon as she heard the news, she wrote to her colleagues."

ADVERB CLAUSES OF PLACE

Adverb clauses of place indicate where the main clause action occurs, introduced by "where" or "wherever":

"Where the river meets the sea, there is a harbor."
"Wherever she went, she found friends."
"She went where the work was needed."

ADVERB CLAUSES OF MANNER

Adverb clauses of manner indicate how the main clause action occurs, introduced by "as," "as if," "as though":

"She spoke as one who has authority."
"He acted as if he were the owner."
"She worked as though her life depended on it."

ADVERB CLAUSES OF CAUSE

Adverb clauses of cause indicate why the main clause action occurs, introduced by "because," "since," "as," "for":

"Because she was tired, she rested."
"Since you asked, I will explain."
"As it was late, we departed."
"She was content, for her work was done."

ADVERB CLAUSES OF PURPOSE

Adverb clauses of purpose indicate the intended result of the main clause action, introduced by "that," "so that," "in order that," "lest":

"She studied that she might pass."
"He spoke loudly so that all could hear."
"They departed early in order that they might arrive before dark."
"He worked carefully lest he make an error."

ADVERB CLAUSES OF RESULT

Adverb clauses of result indicate the actual result of the main clause action, introduced by "so...that," "such...that":

"She was so tired that she fell asleep immediately."
"It was such a beautiful day that they decided to walk."
"He spoke so softly that no one could hear."

ADVERB CLAUSES OF CONDITION

Adverb clauses of condition indicate the circumstances under which the main clause action will occur, introduced by "if," "unless," "provided that," "in case," "suppose," "as long as":

"If you study, you will succeed."
"Unless you hurry, you will be late."
"Provided that the weather is fair, we shall proceed."
"In case of difficulty, consult the manual."
"Suppose you lost everything; what would you do?"
"As long as you are willing, I will help."

ADVERB CLAUSES OF CONCESSION

Adverb clauses of concession indicate that the main clause action occurs despite something, introduced by "although," "though," "even though," "while," "whereas":

"Although she was tired, she continued working."
"Though the task was difficult, she completed it."
"Even though he was wealthy, he lived simply."
"While I agree in principle, I have reservations."
"Whereas some opposed the plan, others supported it."

ADVERB CLAUSES OF COMPARISON

Adverb clauses of comparison indicate the degree or manner of the main clause action relative to something else, introduced by "than," "as":

"She is wiser than her sister is."
"He is not so tall as his brother is."
"She worked as hard as she could."

CHAPTER V: REDUCED ADVERB CLAUSES

Adverb clauses may be reduced to phrases, creating more concise constructions while retaining the semantic relationship.

REDUCED TO PARTICIPIAL PHRASES

When the subject of the adverb clause is the same as the subject of the main clause, the clause may be reduced to a participial phrase:

Time: "Having finished the work, she departed." (From "After she had finished")
Cause: "Being tired, she rested." (From "Because she was tired")
Condition: "Given the circumstances, the decision was understandable." (From "If the circumstances were given")

REDUCED TO VERBLESS CLAUSES

Certain adverb clauses may be reduced to verbless clauses, containing a subject and perhaps a modifier but no verb:

"When in Rome, do as the Romans do." (From "When you are in Rome")
"If possible, arrive early." (From "If it is possible")
"Though tired, she continued." (From "Though she was tired")

CHAPTER VI: ON THE PLACEMENT OF SUBORDINATE CLAUSES

Subordinate clauses may be placed at the beginning, middle, or end of the sentence, the placement affecting emphasis and rhythm.

INITIAL POSITION

Placing the subordinate clause at the beginning gives it prominence and sets up expectations:

"When she arrived, she found the manuscript waiting."
"Although the task was difficult, she completed it."
"If you consent, I will proceed."

The initial subordinate clause creates a periodic structure, withholding the main assertion until the end and thus giving it emphasis.

MEDIAL POSITION

Placing the subordinate clause in the middle interrupts the main clause, creating a parenthetical effect:

"She, having discovered the manuscript, became famous."
"The manuscript, which had been lost for centuries, was found."
"The scholar, though exhausted, continued her work."

The medial position gives the subordinate information a secondary, almost incidental character.

FINAL POSITION

Placing the subordinate clause at the end gives the main clause prominence, the subordinate information following as an afterthought or elaboration:

"She found the manuscript waiting when she arrived."
"She completed the task although it was difficult."
"I will proceed if you consent."

The final position creates a loose structure, presenting the main assertion first and then adding qualification or context.

CHAPTER VII: ON THE CHOICE BETWEEN COORDINATION AND SUBORDINATION

The choice between coordination and subordination reflects a judgment about the relative importance of ideas and about the relationship that should obtain between them.

EQUAL IMPORTANCE

When two ideas are of equal importance, coordination presents them as such:
"The scholar discovered the manuscript, and the assistant preserved it."

Here, both actions are presented as equally significant.

UNEQUAL IMPORTANCE

When one idea is subordinate to another, subordination presents the relationship appropriately:
"The scholar discovered the manuscript, which the assistant preserved."

Here, the discovery is presented as the main assertion; the preservation is presented as additional information about the manuscript.

CAUSAL RELATIONSHIP

When one idea causes or explains another, subordination makes the relationship explicit:
"She studied diligently because she wanted to succeed."

Coordination would leave the relationship implicit:
"She studied diligently, for she wanted to succeed."
"She studied diligently, and she succeeded."

TEMPORAL RELATIONSHIP

When one idea precedes or follows another in time, subordination makes the temporal relationship explicit:
"She departed before he arrived."

Coordination would present the events without explicit temporal relationship:
"She departed, and he arrived later."

The skilled writer chooses between coordination and subordination according to the meaning to be conveyed, the emphasis to be achieved, and the rhythm to be created. The mastery of subordination marks the capacity for complex expression, for the hierarchical organization of thought, and for the construction of discourse that mirrors the intricate relationships among ideas.
