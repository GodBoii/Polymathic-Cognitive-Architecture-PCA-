A COMPREHENSIVE TREATISE ON SYNTAX: THE RULES OF CONCORD AND GOVERNMENT

CHAPTER I: ON THE NATURE OF SYNTAX

Syntax, derived from the Greek "syntaxis" meaning "arrangement," constitutes that branch of grammar which treats of the proper arrangement of words in sentences, according to established usage and the received rules of concord and government. It is through syntax that the raw materials of language—the individual words belonging to the various parts of speech—are fashioned into coherent expressions of thought. Without syntax, words would remain but scattered stones, incapable of forming the edifice of discourse.

The principles of syntax rest upon two fundamental relations: concord, which is the agreement of one word with another in certain grammatical properties; and government, which is the determination by one word of the grammatical form of another. These relations, though seemingly mechanical, serve the profound purpose of ensuring clarity and precision in communication.

CHAPTER II: ON THE RULES OF CONCORD

THE CONCORD OF SUBJECT AND VERB

The most essential rule of concord concerns the agreement between the subject and the verb. The verb must agree with its subject in number and person. A singular subject requires a singular verb; a plural subject requires a plural verb. Thus we say "The scholar writes" but "The scholars write." Similarly, a subject in the first person requires a first-person verb: "I write," "We write"; a subject in the second person requires a second-person verb: "You write"; a subject in the third person requires a third-person verb: "He writes," "They write."

This rule, though simple in statement, admits of numerous complexities in application. When the subject consists of multiple nouns connected by "and," the verb is typically plural: "The master and the servant were present." However, when the connected nouns designate a single entity or idea, the verb remains singular: "Bread and butter is a humble meal." When nouns connected by "and" are preceded by "each," "every," or "no," the verb is singular: "Every man and woman was examined."

When the subject consists of nouns connected by "or" or "nor," the verb agrees with the nearer noun. Thus: "Neither the master nor the servants were present" (plural verb agreeing with "servants"), but "Neither the servants nor the master was present" (singular verb agreeing with "master"). This construction, termed the rule of proximity, demands careful attention.

Collective nouns present particular difficulty. A collective noun, designating a group of individuals—such as "committee," "jury," "family," "audience"—may take either a singular or plural verb according to the sense. When the group is conceived as a unit, the verb is singular: "The committee has decided." When the individuals of the group are separately considered, the verb is plural: "The committee were divided in their opinions."

Nouns that are plural in form but singular in meaning require singular verbs: "The news is encouraging," "Mathematics is a noble science," "Measles is a dangerous disease." Conversely, nouns that are singular in form but plural in meaning take plural verbs: "The people are assembled," "The clergy were present."

THE CONCORD OF PRONOUN AND ANTECEDENT

A pronoun must agree with its antecedent—the noun or noun-equivalent for which it stands—in number, gender, and person. Thus: "The woman arrived; she was weary" (feminine singular pronoun agreeing with feminine singular antecedent). "The men departed; they were silent" (plural pronoun agreeing with plural antecedent).

When the antecedent consists of nouns of different genders connected by "and," the pronoun is plural and, where applicable, masculine: "The father and mother came; they were anxious." When nouns of different genders are connected by "or" or "nor," the pronoun agrees with the nearer antecedent: "Neither the uncle nor his niece was present at her home."

The indefinite pronouns present special considerations. "Each," "either," "neither," "one," "anyone," "everyone," "someone," "nobody," and similar words, though seemingly implying plurality, are grammatically singular and require singular pronouns: "Each of the students has his or her own book." In contemporary usage, the singular "they" is increasingly accepted for such constructions: "Each of the students has their own book."

THE CONCORD OF ADJECTIVE AND NOUN

In English, adjectives exhibit no variation for number, gender, or case; thus the concord of adjective and noun is less elaborate than in certain other languages. However, demonstrative adjectives—"this" and "that," "these" and "those"—must agree with their nouns in number: "this book," "these books," "that manuscript," "those manuscripts."

CHAPTER III: ON THE RULES OF GOVERNMENT

THE GOVERNMENT OF VERBS

Verbs exercise government over their objects. A transitive verb governs its object in the objective case. Thus, in "She invited him," the verb "invited" governs the pronoun "him," which appears in the objective case rather than the nominative "he." This principle extends to all transitive verbs and their objects.

Certain verbs govern particular cases or constructions. The verb "to be," being intransitive, does not govern an object but rather takes a subject complement in the nominative case: "It is I" (not "It is me"). However, in colloquial usage, the objective case has become common: "It is me." Formal discourse retains the nominative.

Verbs of naming, appointing, and declaring may govern two objects—a direct object and an object complement: "They named him successor." Here "him" is the direct object, and "successor" is the object complement.

THE GOVERNMENT OF PREPOSITIONS

Prepositions govern their objects in the objective case. Thus: "with him" (not "with he"), "to her" (not "to she"), "for us" (not "for we"). This rule admits no exception in standard English. The prepositional phrase, consisting of the preposition and its object, may itself be governed by a verb, adjective, or another preposition.

Certain prepositions govern particular cases in constructions that have become fixed in the language. "Between" governs the objective case: "between you and me." "Let" and other imperative verbs are followed by the objective case: "Let him go."

THE GOVERNMENT OF CONJUNCTIONS

Conjunctions, properly speaking, do not govern cases; rather, they connect words or constructions that are already in the appropriate case. However, certain conjunctions introduce clauses that require particular moods. "That," introducing a noun clause, typically follows verbs of commanding, requesting, or advising, and the verb in the subordinate clause appears in the subjunctive mood: "I suggest that he be present." "If" and "unless" may introduce conditions requiring the subjunctive: "If I were wealthy, I would travel."

CHAPTER IV: ON THE SYNTAX OF VERBS

THE TENSES AND THEIR SEQUENCE

The tenses of the verb indicate the time of the action or state relative to the moment of speaking. The present tense denotes present time: "She writes." The past tense denotes past time: "She wrote." The future tense denotes future time: "She will write." The perfect tenses denote completed action: present perfect ("She has written"), past perfect ("She had written"), future perfect ("She will have written").

The sequence of tenses governs the relationship between the tenses in principal and subordinate clauses. When the principal verb is in a past tense, the subordinate verb must also be in a past tense: "He said that he was tired" (not "He said that he is tired"). When the principal verb is in a present or future tense, the subordinate verb may be in any tense appropriate to the meaning: "He says that he is tired," "He says that he was tired," "He says that he will be tired."

THE MOODS AND THEIR USES

The indicative mood, the most common, states facts or asks questions: "The letter arrived." The imperative mood issues commands or entreaties: "Arrive promptly." The subjunctive mood, increasingly rare in modern English, expresses wishes, conditions contrary to fact, and certain subordinate clauses: "I wish that he were here," "If I were wealthy," "I suggest that he be informed."

The subjunctive mood requires particular attention. In conditions contrary to fact, the past subjunctive is used: "If I knew the answer, I would tell you." The verb "to be" distinguishes the subjunctive from the indicative in this construction: "If I was" (indicative, implying possibility) versus "If I were" (subjunctive, implying impossibility or unreality).

THE VOICES

The active voice represents the subject as performing the action: "The author wrote the book." The passive voice represents the subject as receiving the action: "The book was written by the author." The passive voice is formed by the auxiliary "to be" combined with the past participle of the main verb.

The passive construction is appropriate when the agent of the action is unknown or irrelevant, or when the focus is upon the recipient of the action. However, excessive use of the passive voice may render discourse weak and indirect. The active voice, being more direct and vigorous, is generally preferable.

CHAPTER V: ON THE SYNTAX OF CLAUSES

A clause is a group of words containing a subject and a predicate, forming part of a larger sentence. Clauses are of two kinds: independent (or principal) clauses, which can stand alone as complete sentences; and dependent (or subordinate) clauses, which cannot stand alone but function within the larger sentence.

Dependent clauses are classified according to their function. A noun clause functions as a noun within the sentence: "What he said surprised me." An adjective clause modifies a noun or pronoun: "The book that I read was fascinating." An adverb clause modifies a verb, adjective, or adverb: "When she arrived, we departed."

Adjective clauses, introduced by relative pronouns, require careful attention to case. The relative pronoun takes its case from its function within the subordinate clause, not from its antecedent: "The man who called" (nominative, subject of "called"), "The man whom I called" (objective, object of "called"), "The man whose car I borrowed" (possessive).

Thus do the rules of syntax govern the arrangement of words, ensuring that language serves its noble purpose of conveying thought with clarity, precision, and elegance.
