A COMPREHENSIVE TREATISE ON PUNCTUATION: THE MECHANICS OF WRITTEN DISCOURSE

CHAPTER I: ON THE NATURE AND PURPOSE OF PUNCTUATION

Punctuation, that system of marks whereby the structure of written discourse is indicated, serves purposes analogous to those served in spoken discourse by pauses, intonation, and emphasis. Through punctuation, the writer guides the reader through the structure of the text, indicating where one thought ends and another begins, which elements are coordinate and which subordinate, where emphasis falls and where the voice rises or falls. Punctuation is not merely a matter of correctness but of clarity, of rhetoric, of the very structure of thought as it is rendered in visible form.

The principal marks of punctuation in English are the period, the question mark, the exclamation point, the comma, the semicolon, the colon, the dash, the hyphen, the apostrophe, the quotation marks, and the parentheses. Each serves a distinct function, and each must be understood both in its primary uses and in the conventions that govern its employment.

CHAPTER II: THE PERIOD, QUESTION MARK, AND EXCLAMATION POINT

These three marks, termed terminal punctuation, indicate the end of a sentence and simultaneously signal the nature of the sentence.

THE PERIOD

The period indicates the end of a declarative sentence, a sentence that makes a statement:

"The manuscript lay open upon the table."

The period also follows abbreviations:
"Dr. Smith arrived at 3 p.m."

When an abbreviation ending with a period occurs at the end of a sentence, only one period is used:
"He arrived at 3 p.m." (Not "at 3 p.m..")

THE QUESTION MARK

The question mark indicates the end of an interrogative sentence, a sentence that asks a question:

"Has the manuscript been examined?"

The question mark also follows interrogative elements within sentences:
"She wondered, Has the manuscript been examined? and turned to the expert for confirmation."

When a question is part of a larger statement, the question mark is placed within the quotation marks if the quoted material is a question:
"She asked, "Has the manuscript been examined?""

THE EXCLAMATION POINT

The exclamation point indicates the end of an exclamatory sentence, a sentence that expresses strong emotion:

"What a remarkable discovery!"

The exclamation point may also follow interjections and emphatic expressions:
"Alas! The manuscript has been damaged."
"Stop! Do not touch it."

The exclamation point should be used sparingly in formal writing, reserved for genuine exclamations.

CHAPTER III: THE COMMA

The comma, that most versatile and most frequently misused mark of punctuation, serves to indicate the structure of sentences by marking the boundaries between elements. The comma indicates a separation that is less complete than that indicated by a period or semicolon, yet sufficient to prevent confusion.

THE COMMA IN SERIES

The comma separates items in a series of three or more:

"The manuscript was ancient, valuable, and rare."

The final comma in a series, termed the serial comma or Oxford comma, is optional but recommended for clarity. Without it, ambiguity may arise:
"She dedicated the work to her parents, the author and the editor." (Ambiguous: are the parents the author and editor?)
"She dedicated the work to her parents, the author, and the editor." (Clear: three dedicatees)

THE COMMA WITH COORDINATE ADJECTIVES

The comma separates coordinate adjectives—adjectives that each modify the noun independently:

"She studied the ancient, valuable, rare manuscript."

When adjectives are not coordinate—when one modifies the noun phrase formed by the other adjective and the noun—no comma is used:
"She studied the ancient manuscript collection." (Not "ancient, manuscript collection")

The test for coordination: if "and" can be inserted between the adjectives, they are coordinate and require a comma.

THE COMMA IN COMPOUND SENTENCES

The comma precedes a coordinating conjunction that joins independent clauses:

"The manuscript was ancient, and it was remarkably well preserved."

When the independent clauses are short and closely connected, the comma may be omitted:
"She studied and she wrote."

THE COMMA AFTER INTRODUCTORY ELEMENTS

The comma follows introductory elements—words, phrases, or clauses that precede the main clause:

Introductory word:
"However, the evidence was inconclusive."
"Therefore, she proceeded with caution."

Introductory phrase:
"In the morning, she began her work."
"Having finished the translation, she turned to the commentary."

Introductory clause:
"When she arrived, the meeting had already begun."
"Although the task was difficult, she completed it successfully."

Short introductory elements may be followed without a comma when no ambiguity results:
"Yesterday she began her work."
"In 1847 the manuscript was discovered."

THE COMMA WITH NONRESTRICTIVE ELEMENTS

The comma sets off nonrestrictive elements—elements that add information but do not limit or define the noun they modify:

"The scholar, who had spent decades in research, was finally recognized."
"The manuscript, which had been lost for centuries, was remarkably preserved."

Restrictive elements—elements that limit or define—are not set off by commas:
"The scholar who discovered the manuscript was renowned."
"The manuscript that she discovered was ancient."

THE COMMA WITH APPOSITIVES

The comma sets off nonrestrictive appositives—nouns that rename another noun:

"The manuscript, a rare codex from the ninth century, attracted considerable attention."
"Her discovery, a complete surprise to her colleagues, changed the field."

Restrictive appositives are not set off:
"The scholar Smith made the discovery."
"The year 1847 marked a turning point."

THE COMMA WITH PARENTHETICAL ELEMENTS

The comma sets off parenthetical elements—elements that interrupt the flow of the sentence:

"The manuscript, it must be admitted, was in poor condition."
"Her discovery, however significant, raised more questions than it answered."

THE COMMA IN DIRECT ADDRESS

The comma sets off the name or title of the person addressed:

"Professor, have you examined the manuscript?"
"Have you examined the manuscript, Professor?"

THE COMMA WITH QUOTATIONS

The comma separates a direct quotation from the introductory expression:

"She said, "The manuscript is authentic.""
"The manuscript is authentic," she said.

When the quotation is integrated into the sentence structure, no comma is used:
"She said that the manuscript was authentic."

THE COMMA IN ADDRESSES AND DATES

The comma separates elements in addresses and dates:

"She lived at 123 Main Street, Springfield, Illinois, from 1990 to 2000."
"The manuscript was discovered on June 15, 1847, in the archive."

CHAPTER IV: THE SEMICOLON

The semicolon indicates a separation that is more complete than that indicated by the comma but less complete than that indicated by the period. It joins elements that are closely related in meaning but grammatically independent.

THE SEMICOLON BETWEEN INDEPENDENT CLAUSES

The semicolon joins independent clauses not connected by a coordinating conjunction:

"The manuscript was ancient; its pages were yellowed with age."

The semicolon is particularly appropriate when the clauses are closely connected in meaning:
"She studied diligently; she passed the examination."

THE SEMICOLON WITH CONJUNCTIVE ADVERBS

The semicolon precedes a conjunctive adverb that connects independent clauses:

"The manuscript was valuable; however, its authenticity was uncertain."
"She studied diligently; consequently, she passed the examination."

Common conjunctive adverbs include: "however," "therefore," "consequently," "moreover," "furthermore," "nevertheless," "nonetheless," "accordingly," "thus," "hence," "besides," "meanwhile."

THE SEMICOLON IN SERIES

The semicolon separates items in a series when the items themselves contain commas:

"The committee included three scholars: Professor Smith, the chairman; Professor Jones, the secretary; and Professor Brown, the treasurer."
"She visited three cities: Paris, France; London, England; and Rome, Italy."

CHAPTER V: THE COLON

The colon indicates that what follows amplifies, explains, or fulfills what precedes. It serves as an introduction, directing attention forward.

THE COLON BEFORE A LIST

The colon introduces a list or enumeration:

"Three items were required: paper, ink, and quills."
"The archive contained several treasures: manuscripts, letters, and rare books."

The colon is typically preceded by a complete clause:
"She needed three items: paper, ink, and quills."

THE COLON BEFORE AN EXPLANATION

The colon introduces an explanation or amplification:

"The reason was clear: the manuscript had been misattributed."
"She faced a dilemma: she could not both tell the truth and spare his feelings."

THE COLON BEFORE A QUOTATION

The colon introduces a formal quotation:

"The scholar declared: "This manuscript changes everything we thought we knew.""
"She wrote in her journal: "The discovery has exceeded all expectations.""

THE COLON IN TIME EXPRESSIONS

The colon separates hours from minutes in time expressions:

"The meeting began at 3:15 p.m."
"The train departed at 9:30 a.m."

THE COLON IN TITLES AND CITATIONS

The colon separates title from subtitle and place from publisher in citations:

"The Art of Translation: Principles and Practice"
"Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2020."

CHAPTER VI: THE DASH

The dash indicates a more emphatic separation than the comma, setting off elements with greater prominence. The dash may be rendered as an em dash (—) or, in informal writing, as two hyphens (--).

THE DASH FOR EMPHATIC INTERRUPTION

The dash sets off parenthetical elements with emphasis:

"The manuscript—discovered after centuries of searching—proved to be authentic."
"Her discovery—that rare combination of luck and diligence—transformed the field."

THE DASH FOR AN ABRUPT BREAK

The dash indicates an abrupt break in thought:

"The manuscript was authentic—or so it seemed until further examination revealed—"
"She believed the manuscript was genuine, but—"

THE DASH BEFORE A SUMMARIZING STATEMENT

The dash introduces a summarizing or explanatory statement:

"Paper, ink, quills—these were the tools of her trade."
"Ancient manuscripts, rare books, historical letters—all these attracted her to the archive."

THE DASH IN DIALOGUE

The dash indicates interrupted speech in dialogue:

"But I thought—"
"You thought wrong," he interrupted.

CHAPTER VII: THE HYPHEN

The hyphen joins elements that function together as a unit. It differs from the dash in being shorter and in serving a connective rather than a separative function.

THE HYPHEN IN COMPOUND WORDS

The hyphen joins elements in compound words:

Compound nouns: "mother-in-law," "passer-by," "editor-in-chief"
Compound adjectives: "well-known," "long-term," "nineteenth-century"
Compound verbs: "to baby-sit," "to double-check," "to cross-examine"

THE HYPHEN WITH PREFIXES AND SUFFIXES

The hyphen joins prefixes to proper nouns and to words beginning with the same vowel:
"pre-Columbian," "un-American," "re-enter," "co-operate"

THE HYPHEN IN NUMBERS AND FRACTIONS

The hyphen joins the parts of spelled-out numbers from twenty-one through ninety-nine:
"twenty-one," "thirty-five," "ninety-nine"

Fractions used as adjectives are hyphenated:
"two-thirds majority," "one-half portion"

THE HYPHEN TO AVOID AMBIGUITY

The hyphen distinguishes compound words from similar phrases:
"re-cover" (cover again) vs. "recover" (regain)
"re-sign" (sign again) vs. "resign" (quit)

THE HYPHEN AT LINE BREAKS

The hyphen divides words at the end of a line, breaking between syllables:
"man-u-script," "dis-cov-er-y," "val-u-a-ble"

CHAPTER VIII: THE APOSTROPHE

The apostrophe indicates possession or omission.

THE APOSTROPHE IN POSSESSIVES

The apostrophe indicates possession, with placement depending on the form of the noun:

Singular nouns: add apostrophe and s
"the scholar's manuscript," "the woman's discovery," "the child's book"

Plural nouns ending in s: add apostrophe only
"the scholars' manuscripts," "the women's discoveries," "the children's books"

Plural nouns not ending in s: add apostrophe and s
"the children's books," "the people's choice"

Singular nouns ending in s: add apostrophe and s or apostrophe only
"Jones's book" or "Jones' book" (both acceptable)

Indefinite pronouns: add apostrophe and s
"anyone's guess," "someone's book," "nobody's business"

THE APOSTROPHE IN CONTRACTIONS

The apostrophe indicates the omission of letters in contractions:

"can't" (cannot), "don't" (do not), "won't" (will not)
"it's" (it is), "they're" (they are), "you're" (you are)
"I've" (I have), "she'd" (she would/had), "we'll" (we will)

The distinction between "it's" (contraction of "it is") and "its" (possessive pronoun) merits particular attention:
"It's a valuable manuscript." (It is)
"Its value is considerable." (Possessive)

THE APOSTROPHE IN PLURALS

The apostrophe is generally not used in forming plurals of nouns:
"manuscripts" (not "manuscript's"), "scholars" (not "scholar's")

The apostrophe may be used in forming plurals of letters, numbers, and symbols when necessary for clarity:
"Mind your p's and q's."
"The 1960's" (or "the 1960s"—both acceptable).

CHAPTER IX: QUOTATION MARKS

Quotation marks enclose direct quotations and certain titles.

DOUBLE AND SINGLE QUOTATION MARKS

In American usage, double quotation marks enclose direct quotations; single quotation marks enclose quotations within quotations:

"She said, "The manuscript is authentic.""
"She reported, "The scholar said, 'The manuscript is authentic.'"

In British usage, the convention is typically reversed, with single quotation marks for primary quotations.

QUOTATION MARKS WITH DIRECT QUOTATIONS

Quotation marks enclose the exact words of a speaker or writer:

"She said, "The manuscript is authentic.""
"The scholar wrote, "This discovery changes everything.""

When a quotation is interrupted, quotation marks enclose each part:
"The manuscript," she said, "is authentic."

QUOTATION MARKS WITH TITLES

Quotation marks enclose titles of shorter works—articles, essays, short stories, poems, songs, episodes:

"She read "The Library of Babel" by Borges."
"The episode "The Scholar's Discovery" was particularly compelling."

Titles of longer works—books, films, plays, albums—are typically italicized rather than enclosed in quotation marks.

QUOTATION MARKS FOR SPECIAL TERMS

Quotation marks may enclose terms used in a special or technical sense, or terms being defined:

"The term "manuscript" derives from the Latin for "written by hand.""
"She used the word "authentic" in a technical sense."

QUOTATION MARKS WITH PUNCTUATION

In American usage, periods and commas are placed inside quotation marks, regardless of the original text:

"She called the manuscript "authentic," though she had doubts."
"She wrote, "The manuscript is authentic."

Colons and semicolons are placed outside quotation marks:
"She called the manuscript "authentic"; however, she had doubts.

Question marks and exclamation points are placed inside or outside according to the sense:
"She asked, "Is the manuscript authentic?" (The quotation is a question)
"Did she say, "The manuscript is authentic"? (The sentence is a question)

CHAPTER X: PARENTHESES AND BRACKETS

Parentheses and brackets enclose elements that are supplementary or editorial.

PARENTHESES

Parentheses enclose supplementary or explanatory material:

"The manuscript (discovered in 1847) was remarkably preserved."
"She studied the manuscript carefully (as she had been trained to do) before rendering her judgment."

Parentheses may also enclose enumerations:
"The archive contained three types of documents: (1) manuscripts, (2) letters, and (3) rare books."

Parentheses enclose citations and references:
"The manuscript was discovered in 1847 (Smith 2020)."

BRACKETS

Brackets enclose editorial insertions within quotations:

"She wrote that the manuscript was "discovered [in the archive] by a scholar.""
"He reported that "she [the scholar] had made a remarkable discovery."

Brackets may also enclose corrections:
"The manuscript was "discovered in 1847 [1848?]."

Thus through punctuation does written discourse achieve the structure and clarity that spoken discourse achieves through voice and pause. The mastery of punctuation marks the capacity for written expression that is not only correct but clear, not only clear but elegant, guiding the reader through the structure of thought with precision and grace.
