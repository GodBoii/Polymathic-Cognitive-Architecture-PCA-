A TREATISE ON SENTENCE PATTERNS AND THEIR VARIETIES: THE ARCHITECTURE OF THOUGHT

CHAPTER I: ON THE SIMPLE SENTENCE AND ITS PATTERNS

The simple sentence, though elementary in nomenclature, admits of considerable variety in construction. It consists of but one subject and one predicate, yet either element may be modified, expanded, or compounded, yielding patterns of great flexibility and expressive power.

PATTERN THE FIRST: Subject—Verb

The most fundamental pattern comprises a subject and an intransitive verb, requiring no complement to complete its meaning. The subject performs the action, and the verb expresses that action without transferring it to any object.

Examples:
"The night fell."
"Silence reigned."
"The traveler departed."
"Birds sing."

This pattern, though simple, may be enriched by adverbial modifiers: "The night fell swiftly." "Silence reigned over the abandoned hall." Yet the essential structure remains unchanged—subject and verb alone.

PATTERN THE SECOND: Subject—Verb—Subject Complement

When the verb is a linking verb—such as "to be," "to seem," "to appear," "to become," "to remain"—the predicate requires a subject complement to complete the thought. The complement, typically a noun or adjective, ascribes some quality or identity to the subject.

Examples:
"The stranger was a physician."
"The garden remains beautiful."
"She became anxious."
"The argument seemed sound."

The linking verb serves as a bridge between the subject and its complement, the latter being in the nominative case when a noun or pronoun. Thus we say "It is I" rather than "It is me," though colloquial usage increasingly favors the latter.

PATTERN THE THIRD: Subject—Verb—Direct Object

The transitive verb, by its nature, transfers action from the subject to an object. This pattern, among the most common in English, comprises subject, transitive verb, and direct object.

Examples:
"The author wrote a manuscript."
"She composed a symphony."
"They built a cathedral."
"He solved the problem."

The direct object, receiving the action of the verb, appears in the objective case. Pronouns thus take their objective form: "She saw him," not "She saw he."

PATTERN THE FOURTH: Subject—Verb—Indirect Object—Direct Object

Certain transitive verbs—those of giving, telling, showing, and similar meanings—admit of two objects: an indirect object, designating the recipient of the action, and a direct object, designating that which is given, told, or shown.

Examples:
"The merchant sold the customer a ring."
"She taught the students grammar."
"He gave his friend a book."
"They showed the visitors the garden."

The indirect object typically precedes the direct object without a preposition. However, the construction may be recast with the indirect object placed after the direct object, governed by "to" or "for": "The merchant sold a ring to the customer." "She taught grammar to the students."

PATTERN THE FIFTH: Subject—Verb—Direct Object—Object Complement

Verbs of naming, appointing, considering, and similar meanings require not only a direct object but also an object complement, which ascribes some quality or identity to the object.

Examples:
"The council named him chairman."
"They considered the matter settled."
"She found the room empty."
"We painted the door green."

The object complement may be a noun, as in the first example, or an adjective, as in the others. This pattern, though less common than the preceding, proves invaluable for certain types of expression.

CHAPTER II: ON THE COMPOUND SENTENCE

The compound sentence joins together two or more independent clauses, each capable of standing alone as a complete sentence. The connection is effected by coordinating conjunctions—"and," "but," "or," "nor," "for," "yet," "so"—or by semicolons, with or without conjunctive adverbs.

THE COORDINATING CONJUNCTIONS

"And" indicates addition or continuation: "The sun set, and the stars emerged."
"But" indicates contrast or exception: "She studied diligently, but she failed the examination."
"Or" indicates alternative: "You must work, or you will suffer want."
"Nor" indicates negative addition: "He would not consent, nor would he refuse."
"For" indicates cause or reason: "She wept, for her heart was heavy."
"Yet" indicates contrast with an element of surprise: "The path was steep, yet they pressed onward."
"So" indicates result: "The storm abated, so we continued our journey."

THE SEMICOLON CONSTRUCTION

The semicolon, that elegant mark of punctuation, joins independent clauses without a conjunction, the connection being implied rather than expressed:

"The rain ceased; the sun emerged."
"She spoke softly; her words carried great weight."
"He declined the offer; his conscience would not permit acceptance."

Conjunctive adverbs—"however," "therefore," "consequently," "moreover," "nevertheless," "furthermore"—may follow the semicolon, clarifying the relationship between the clauses:

"The evidence was compelling; however, the jury remained unconvinced."
"The task was arduous; nevertheless, they completed it."

CHAPTER III: ON THE COMPLEX SENTENCE

The complex sentence contains one independent clause and at least one dependent clause. The dependent clause, introduced by a subordinating conjunction or a relative pronoun, functions as a noun, adjective, or adverb within the sentence.

NOUN CLAUSES

A noun clause serves the same functions as a noun: subject, object, subject complement, or object of a preposition.

As subject: "What she said surprised everyone."
As object: "I know that he is innocent."
As subject complement: "The truth is that we have failed."
As object of a preposition: "She listened to what he advised."

Noun clauses are introduced by "that," "what," "who," "whom," "whose," "which," "where," "when," "why," "how," "whether," "if," and occasionally other words.

ADJECTIVE CLAUSES

An adjective clause modifies a noun or pronoun, typically following it directly. It is introduced by a relative pronoun—"who," "whom," "whose," "which," "that"—or by a relative adverb—"where," "when," "why."

Restrictive (or essential) adjective clauses limit or define the noun they modify, providing information essential to identification: "The book that I borrowed is on the desk." The clause "that I borrowed" specifies which book is meant.

Nonrestrictive (or nonessential) adjective clauses provide additional information about the noun, enclosed by commas: "The book, which I borrowed from the library, is on the desk." The clause adds information but does not identify the book.

The distinction between restrictive and nonrestrictive clauses affects the choice of relative pronoun. "That" is preferred for restrictive clauses; "which" is standard for nonrestrictive clauses. "Who" and "whom" serve for both types when referring to persons.

ADVERB CLAUSES

An adverb clause modifies a verb, adjective, or adverb, indicating time, place, manner, cause, purpose, condition, concession, or comparison.

Time: "When she arrived, the meeting had ended."
Place: "Where the river bends, there is a deep pool."
Manner: "He spoke as if he were an expert."
Cause: "Because she was tired, she retired early."
Purpose: "She studied hard so that she might succeed."
Condition: "If you consent, I will proceed."
Concession: "Although he was wealthy, he lived simply."
Comparison: "She is taller than her sister is."

Adverb clauses are introduced by subordinating conjunctions appropriate to the relationship expressed: "when," "where," "as," "because," "so that," "if," "although," "than," and numerous others.

CHAPTER IV: ON THE COMPOUND-COMPLEX SENTENCE

The compound-complex sentence combines the structures previously discussed, containing at least two independent clauses and at least one dependent clause. This pattern, the most elaborate of all, permits the expression of complex relationships among multiple ideas.

Examples:
"When the storm ended, we emerged from shelter, and we surveyed the damage." (One dependent clause, two independent clauses)
"The letter that arrived yesterday contained important news, but I have not yet read it carefully." (One dependent clause, two independent clauses)
"Because she was exhausted, she retired early; however, she could not sleep." (One dependent clause, two independent clauses joined by semicolon and conjunctive adverb)

The compound-complex sentence demands careful construction, lest it become unwieldy and obscure. The relationships among the clauses must be clear, and the sentence must not exceed the reader's capacity to hold its parts in mind.

CHAPTER V: ON THE PERIODIC AND LOOSE SENTENCE

Beyond the classification by structure, sentences may be distinguished by the arrangement of their elements and the placement of the main idea.

THE PERIODIC SENTENCE

The periodic sentence withholds the main idea until the end, creating suspense and emphasis. The reader cannot grasp the complete meaning until reaching the final words.

Examples:
"Having considered all the evidence, weighed every argument, and deliberated for many hours, the jury finally reached its verdict."
"Though she was weary, though the path was steep, and though the night was dark, she pressed onward to her destination."

The periodic sentence, favored in formal and rhetorical discourse, creates a sense of anticipation and delivers the main idea with force.

THE LOOSE SENTENCE

The loose sentence places the main idea near the beginning, with subordinate elements following. The reader grasps the essential meaning early, and subsequent elements add detail or elaboration.

Examples:
"The jury reached its verdict after considering all the evidence, weighing every argument, and deliberating for many hours."
"She pressed onward to her destination, though she was weary, though the path was steep, and though the night was dark."

The loose sentence, more natural and conversational in tone, is appropriate for narrative and expository writing where clarity and ease of comprehension are paramount.

THE BALANCED SENTENCE

The balanced sentence arranges its elements in parallel structures, creating symmetry and rhythm. This pattern, favored in formal and persuasive discourse, lends elegance and memorability to expression.

Examples:
"She studied the ancient texts; he explored the modern theories."
"To err is human; to forgive, divine."
"The man who speaks much says little; the man who speaks little says much."

Thus through the various patterns of the sentence—simple, compound, complex, and compound-complex; periodic, loose, and balanced—does language accommodate the infinite variety of human thought, providing structures adequate to every purpose of expression.

CHAPTER VI: ON THE CLEFT SENTENCE

The cleft sentence, that deliberate fragmentation of a simple sentence into two parts, serves to emphasize a particular element by isolating it for special attention. The term "cleft" derives from the idea of splitting or dividing—the sentence is cleft in two to foreground a specific component.

THE IT-CLEFT

The it-cleft begins with "it" followed by a form of "to be" and a noun clause introduced by "that" or "who." The element to be emphasized appears between "it is" or "it was" and the noun clause.

**Basic Pattern:** "It is/was + [emphasized element] + that/who + rest of sentence"

**Emphasis on Subject:**
- Normal: "The scholar discovered the manuscript."
- Cleft: "It was the scholar who discovered the manuscript."
- Cleft: "It is the scholar that made this discovery."

**Emphasis on Object:**
- Normal: "She found the ancient text."
- Cleft: "It was the ancient text that she found."
- Cleft: "It is the ancient text she discovered."

**Emphasis on Time:**
- Normal: "The discovery happened in 1847."
- Cleft: "It was in 1847 that the discovery happened."

**Emphasis on Place:**
- Normal: "The manuscript was found in the archive."
- Cleft: "It was in the archive that the manuscript was found."

**Emphasis on Manner:**
- Normal: "She solved the problem through careful analysis."
- Cleft: "It was through careful analysis that she solved the problem."

**Emphasis on Reason:**
- Normal: "He succeeded because of his perseverance."
- Cleft: "It was because of his perseverance that he succeeded."

THE WH-CLEFT (PSEUDO-CLEFT)

The wh-cleft, also termed the pseudo-cleft, uses a wh- clause (typically "what," "who," or "which") to create emphasis. The structure inverts the it-cleft, placing the wh- element at the beginning.

**Basic Pattern:** "What/Who/Which + [verb] + is/was + [rest]"

**Emphasis on Subject:**
- Normal: "The scholar wrote this book."
- Cleft: "What the scholar wrote was this book."
- Cleft: "Who discovered the text was the young assistant."

**Emphasis on Object:**
- Normal: "I want is peace."
- Cleft: "What I want is peace." (Note: singular verb after "what" referring to singular antecedent)

**Emphasis on Action:**
- Normal: "She did was leave."
- Cleft: "What she did was leave." (Note: bare infinitive after "what")

**With "The thing is":**
- "The thing is, we have no time to waste."
- "The thing that matters is the evidence."

FUNCTIONS OF CLEFT SENTENCES

Cleft sentences serve several important functions:

**Emphasis:** They draw attention to the emphasized element:
- "It is not the destination that matters, but the journey."

**Contrast:** They highlight a contrast between two possibilities:
- "It was not the wealth that he sought, but the wisdom."

**Information Structure:** They allow the writer to place given information at the beginning and new information at the end:
- "It was the third chapter that proved most controversial."

**Topicalization:** They enable the speaker to promote an element to the topic position:
- "It was the professor's lecture that everyone remembered."

RESTRICTIONS ON CLEFT SENTENCES

Certain elements cannot be clefted:

- Quantifiers alone: *"It was many that came"* (ungrammatical)
- Predicate adjectives: *"It is red the car is"* (ungrammatical - use "The car is red" instead)
- Certain adverbs: *"It is slowly that he works"* (marginal - better: "He works slowly")

EXAMPLES IN CONTEXT

"The discovery was made in the desert."
→ "It was in the desert that the discovery was made."
→ "What was discovered in the desert was an ancient manuscript."

"The scholar translated the text."
→ "It was the scholar who translated the text."
→ "What the scholar did was translate the text."

CHAPTER VII: ON THE EXISTENTIAL THERE CONSTRUCTION

The existential "there" serves a unique grammatical function: it introduces a new entity into discourse, presenting information as if the reader or listener should become aware of something for the first time. Unlike the demonstrative "there" (meaning "in that place"), the existential "there" carries no deictic meaning but rather signals the presence or existence of something.

THE BASIC EXISTENTIAL STRUCTURE

The existential construction follows this pattern:
"There + [form of "be"] + [subject] + [complement/location]"

**Simple Existence:**
- "There is a book on the table."
- "There are many books in the library."
- "There was a storm last night."

**With Indefinite Subjects:**
- "There is someone at the door."
- "There is something wrong with this picture."
- "There were few people at the meeting."

**With Quantifiers:**
- "There is much to be done."
- "There are several options available."
- "There were many reasons for his departure."

EXISTENTIAL THERE WITH COMPLEX SUBJECTS

The subject following "there" is often indefinite, meaning it refers to something not previously identified in the discourse.

**With Adjectives:**
- "There is a strange man outside."
- "There are many beautiful manuscripts in the collection."
- "There was an urgent message waiting."

**With Participles:**
- "There is a letter being written."
- "There were students studying in the library."
- "There has been a decision made."

**With Noun Phrases:**
- "There is no time to waste."
- "There are three main points to consider."
- "There was no hope of recovery."

EXISTENTIAL THERE WITH PROGRESSIVE ASPECT

**With Present Progressive:**
- "There is someone waiting for you."
- "There are changes happening in the organization."

**With Past Progressive:**
- "There was a storm developing in the distance."
- "There were negotiations taking place."

EXISTENTIAL THERE WITH PERFECT ASPECT

**With Present Perfect:**
- "There has been an accident on the highway."
- "There have been several complaints filed."

**With Past Perfect:**
- "There had been no indication of trouble."
- "There had been a meeting scheduled before the cancellation."

EXISTENTIAL THERE WITH MODAL VERBS

- "There could be a solution to this problem."
- "There might be someone who knows the answer."
- "There must be a reason for this behavior."
- "There will be consequences to face."

EXISTENTIAL THERE WITH SEMI-MODALS

- "There used to be a castle here."
- "There ought to be a law against this."
- "There seems to be a problem."

NEGATIVE EXISTENTIAL CONSTRUCTIONS

The negative existential requires careful construction:

**With "no" (not "not a"):**
- "There is no book on the table." (correct)
- "There is not a book on the table." (less common, different meaning)

**With Negative Indefinites:**
- "There is nothing in the room."
- "There is no one at home."
- "There is nowhere to go."
- "There is no reason to worry."

**With "not...any":**
- "There isn't any milk left."
- "There aren't any students in the classroom."

**Contracted Forms:**
- "There's" (there is/has)
- "There're" (there are) - informal
- "There'll" (there will)
- "There'd" (there would/had)

EXISTENTIAL THERE WITH ADVERBIAL EXPRESSIONS

**Place:**
- "There is a book here."
- "There are flowers everywhere."
- "There was a noise downstairs."

**Time:**
- "There is a meeting today."
- "There was a time when kings ruled."
- "There will be a moment for questions."

**Manner/Reason:**
- "There is a way to solve this."
- "There is no reason to complain."

EXISTENTIAL THERE IN QUESTIONS

**Yes/No Questions:**
- "Is there a problem?"
- "Are there any questions?"
- "Was there a message for me?"

**Wh- Questions:**
- "What's in the box?"
- "Who's at the door?"
- "What's the matter?"

**Tag Questions:**
- "There's a meeting today, isn't there?"
- "There were some issues, weren't there?"

EXISTENTIAL THERE WITH RELATIVE CLAUSES

- "There is someone who can help you."
- "There is nothing that I can do."
- "There are several reasons why this happened."

EXISTENTIAL THERE WITH APPOSITIVES

- "There is a theory, one that explains everything."
- "There is the fact, however, that time is running out."

FUNCTIONS IN DISCOURSE

**Introducing New Information:**
- "A man entered the room. There was a look of fear on his face."

**Creating Suspense:**
- "There is a secret I must tell you."

**Presenting Assumptions:**
- "There is no doubt that he is guilty."

**Establishing Context:**
- "Once upon a time, there lived a king in a distant land."

**Highlighting Existence:**
- "There is no alternative."

IDIOMATIC USES

- "There you are!" (found you)
- "There we go!" (success/approval)
- "There, there." (comfort)
- "There's a good boy/girl." (praise)
- "There it is." (pointing something out)
- "There you go again." (repetition)

COMMON ERRORS TO AVOID

**Wrong:**
- *"There is many reasons."* (subject-verb disagreement)
- *"There is no any reason."* (double negative)

**Correct:**
- "There are many reasons."
- "There is no reason." or "There isn't any reason."

Thus through cleft sentences and existential constructions does language provide additional means of emphasis, information structure, and discourse organization, enabling speakers to highlight particular elements, introduce new entities, and shape the flow of information in ways that simple sentences cannot accomplish.
