A TREATISE ON VOICE, MOOD, AND CONDITIONAL CONSTRUCTIONS: THE MODALITIES OF LANGUAGE

CHAPTER I: ON THE NATURE OF VOICE

Voice, that grammatical category whereby the relationship between the subject and the action is indicated, constitutes one of the fundamental distinctions in verb morphology. Through voice, the speaker determines whether the subject is presented as the agent of the action—the doer—or as the recipient of the action—the one acted upon. The choice of voice is not merely grammatical but rhetorical, affecting the emphasis, focus, and tone of the utterance.

English distinguishes two voices: the active voice, wherein the subject performs the action; and the passive voice, wherein the subject receives the action. This binary distinction, though seemingly simple, admits of considerable complexity in application and carries significant implications for the construction of discourse.

CHAPTER II: THE ACTIVE VOICE

The active voice presents the subject as the agent of the action, the doer of the deed. The verb expresses what the subject does, and any object follows as the recipient of the action.

FORM OF THE ACTIVE VOICE

The active voice employs the simple form of the verb, without auxiliary verbs specific to voice:
"She wrote the manuscript."
"He discovered the truth."
"They built the cathedral."

The subject precedes the verb, and any direct object follows.

FUNCTIONS OF THE ACTIVE VOICE

The active voice is the unmarked, default construction in English. It is used when the agent is known, important, or to be emphasized:
"The scholar wrote the manuscript in 1847."
"He discovered the truth after years of research."
"They built the cathedral over several centuries."

The active voice is typically more direct, more concise, and more vigorous than the passive:
Active: "She wrote the manuscript." (Four words)
Passive: "The manuscript was written by her." (Six words)

The active voice is generally preferred in narrative, in argumentative writing, and in any context where clarity and directness are paramount.

CHAPTER III: THE PASSIVE VOICE

The passive voice presents the subject as the recipient of the action, the one acted upon. The agent, if expressed at all, appears in a prepositional phrase introduced by "by."

FORM OF THE PASSIVE VOICE

The passive voice is formed with the auxiliary "be" followed by the past participle of the main verb. The tense is indicated by the form of "be":

Present passive: "The manuscript is written in ancient script."
Past passive: "The manuscript was written in 1847."
Future passive: "The manuscript will be written next year."
Present perfect passive: "The manuscript has been written."
Past perfect passive: "The manuscript had been written before the fire."
Modal passive: "The manuscript must be written carefully."

The agent, if expressed, follows in a "by" phrase:
"The manuscript was written by the scholar."

FUNCTIONS OF THE PASSIVE VOICE

The passive voice serves several important functions:

Agent Unknown: When the agent is unknown, the passive allows the action to be expressed without specifying who performed it:
"The manuscript was discovered in 1847." (Agent unknown)
"The decision was made yesterday." (Agent unspecified)

Agent Unimportant: When the agent is less important than the recipient, the passive focuses attention on the recipient:
"The cathedral was built in the thirteenth century." (Focus on cathedral)
"The treaty was signed in 1847." (Focus on treaty)

Receiving End Focus: When the speaker wishes to focus on the recipient rather than the agent, the passive places the recipient in subject position:
"The manuscript was examined by experts." (Focus on manuscript)
"The patient was treated by the physician." (Focus on patient)

Scientific and Technical Writing: The passive is common in scientific and technical writing, where the focus is on the process rather than the researcher:
"The solution was heated to boiling."
"The data were analyzed statistically."
"The experiment was repeated three times."

Diplomatic and Official Language: The passive allows actions to be described without attributing them to specific individuals:
"Mistakes were made." (No one blamed specifically)
"The decision was taken after due consideration." (Committee not specified)

THE GET-PASSIVE

In informal English, "get" may replace "be" as the passive auxiliary:
"He got fired last week."
"She got promoted yesterday."
"They got caught in the rain."

The "get"-passive often implies that something happened to the subject, sometimes with negative connotations or with implication of the subject's involvement.

CHAPTER IV: ON THE NATURE OF MOOD

Mood, that grammatical category whereby the speaker's attitude toward the proposition is indicated, constitutes one of the fundamental modalities of language. Through mood, the speaker indicates whether the proposition is presented as fact, as command, as wish, or as hypothesis. The mood of the verb thus reflects not the temporal location of the action but the epistemic and volitional stance of the speaker toward the action.

English distinguishes three moods: the indicative mood, which states facts or asks questions; the imperative mood, which issues commands or entreaties; and the subjunctive mood, which expresses wishes, conditions, and various non-factual notions. This threefold distinction, though partially obscured in modern English by the reduction of distinctive subjunctive forms, remains fundamental to the expression of modality.

CHAPTER V: THE INDICATIVE MOOD

The indicative mood, the unmarked and most common mood, states facts or asks questions. The verb forms previously discussed—all the tenses and aspects—belong to the indicative mood unless otherwise specified.

STATEMENTS OF FACT

The indicative mood states what the speaker presents as fact:
"The manuscript was written in 1847."
"She has studied the subject for years."
"They will complete the work tomorrow."

QUESTIONS

The indicative mood asks questions, seeking information:
"Was the manuscript written in 1847?"
"Has she studied the subject for years?"
"Will they complete the work tomorrow?"

The indicative mood is used when the speaker assumes the reality of the proposition and wishes to assert it or to inquire about it.

CHAPTER VI: THE IMPERATIVE MOOD

The imperative mood issues commands, requests, or entreaties. The imperative typically has no expressed subject, the person addressed being understood.

FORM OF THE IMPERATIVE

The imperative employs the base form of the verb, without a subject:
"Write the manuscript."
"Study the subject carefully."
"Complete the work tomorrow."

The negative imperative employs "do not" or "don't":
"Do not write the manuscript."
"Don't study the subject carelessly."

FUNCTIONS OF THE IMPERATIVE

Commands: The imperative issues direct commands:
"Close the door."
"Submit the report by Friday."
"Remain silent."

Requests: The imperative makes requests, often softened by "please":
"Please close the door."
"Kindly submit the report by Friday."
"Please remain silent."

Entreaties: The imperative expresses entreaties or wishes:
"Help me, I beg you."
"Save us from this fate."
"Have mercy upon us."

Instructions: The imperative gives instructions:
"Turn left at the corner."
"Add the flour gradually."
"Press the button twice."

THE IMPERATIVE WITH "LET"

The construction "let" + object + base form expresses suggestion or permission:
"Let us proceed with caution."
"Let the work begin."
"Let him speak."

THE FIRST PERSON IMPERATIVE

The first person imperative employs "let":
"Let me explain."
"Let us consider the evidence."
"Let us not forget the past."

CHAPTER VII: THE SUBJUNCTIVE MOOD

The subjunctive mood expresses wishes, conditions, and various non-factual notions. Though distinctive subjunctive forms have largely disappeared from modern English, certain constructions retain the subjunctive, and its use remains important in formal discourse.

THE MANDATORY SUBJUNCTIVE

The mandatory subjunctive appears in that-clauses following verbs of demanding, recommending, and requiring. The verb in the subordinate clause appears in the base form, regardless of the subject:

"I demand that he be present."
"She recommended that he study harder."
"They required that she submit the report."
"It is essential that he arrive on time."
"It is important that she be informed."

This construction is termed "mandatory" because it follows verbs and adjectives involving requirement or necessity. The subjunctive form is the base form of the verb: "be," not "is" or "are"; "study," not "studies" or "studied."

THE WERE-SUBJUNCTIVE

The were-subjunctive appears in conditional clauses and in clauses expressing wishes. The verb "to be" appears as "were" regardless of the subject:

"If I were wealthy, I would travel."
"If she were here, she would help."
"I wish that he were present."
"If only the manuscript were authentic."

In formal English, "were" is preferred over "was" in these constructions. In informal English, "was" is common:
Informal: "If I was wealthy, I would travel."
Formal: "If I were wealthy, I would travel."

THE FORMULAIC SUBJUNCTIVE

The formulaic subjunctive appears in fixed expressions:
"God save the Queen."
"Heaven forbid."
"Be that as it may."
"Far be it from me to criticize."
"Suffice it to say."

These expressions retain archaic subjunctive forms that have otherwise disappeared from the language.

CHAPTER VIII: ON CONDITIONAL CONSTRUCTIONS

Conditional constructions express the dependence of one proposition upon another. The condition—introduced by "if," "unless," or similar words—states the circumstance under which the main clause proposition holds true. Conditionals are classified according to the relationship between the condition and the reality.

THE ZERO CONDITIONAL

The zero conditional expresses general truths or laws of nature. Both clauses use the present tense:

"If water freezes, it expands."
"If you heat ice, it melts."
"If she studies, she learns."

The zero conditional indicates that the condition always leads to the result; it expresses a general or universal relationship.

THE FIRST CONDITIONAL

The first conditional expresses real or possible conditions in the future. The if-clause uses the present tense; the main clause uses "will" or another future expression:

"If she studies, she will pass."
"If he arrives on time, we will begin."
"If it rains, the event will be cancelled."

The first conditional indicates that the condition is a real possibility; if the condition is fulfilled, the result will follow.

THE SECOND CONDITIONAL

The second conditional expresses unreal or hypothetical conditions in the present or future. The if-clause uses the past subjunctive (or simple past); the main clause uses "would" or another modal:

"If I were wealthy, I would travel."
"If she studied harder, she would pass."
"If he knew the answer, he would tell us."

The second conditional indicates that the condition is contrary to fact in the present; the result would follow if the condition were true, but it is not.

THE THIRD CONDITIONAL

The third conditional expresses unreal conditions in the past. The if-clause uses the past perfect; the main clause uses "would have" or another modal perfect:

"If she had studied, she would have passed."
"If he had arrived on time, we would have begun."
"If it had not rained, the event would not have been cancelled."

The third conditional indicates that the condition was contrary to fact in the past; the result would have followed if the condition had been true, but it was not.

MIXED CONDITIONALS

Mixed conditionals combine elements from different conditionals:

Past condition, present result:
"If she had studied harder, she would be more successful now."

Present condition, past result:
"If I were not afraid of heights, I would have climbed the mountain."

INVERTED CONDITIONALS

Conditionals may be expressed with inversion, omitting "if":

"Were I wealthy, I would travel." (Instead of "If I were wealthy")
"Had she studied, she would have passed." (Instead of "If she had studied")
"Should he arrive, tell him to wait." (Instead of "If he should arrive")

CONDITIONALS WITHOUT "IF"

Conditionals may be introduced by other words:

"Unless she studies, she will not pass." (Negative condition)
"Provided that she studies, she will pass." (Positive condition)
"Suppose she studied harder; would she pass?" (Hypothetical)
"In case of fire, break the glass." (Conditional phrase)

CHAPTER IX: ON THE RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN VOICE, MOOD, AND CONDITIONALS

Voice, mood, and conditionals interact in complex ways, creating constructions that combine multiple modalities.

PASSIVE CONDITIONALS

Conditionals may be expressed in the passive voice:

"If the manuscript were discovered, it would be studied by scholars."
"If the work had been completed, the results would have been published."
"Were the truth known, justice would be served."

SUBJUNCTIVE IN CONDITIONALS

The subjunctive mood is essential in formal conditionals:

"If I were you, I would accept."
"If he were present, he would object."
"Had the manuscript been authentic, it would have been valuable."

IMPERATIVE IN CONDITIONALS

The imperative may appear in the main clause of a conditional:

"If you see her, tell her I called."
"Should he arrive, show him in."
"If you need help, ask."

Thus through voice, mood, and conditionals does language express the fundamental modalities of human experience—agency and passivity, fact and wish, reality and hypothesis. The mastery of these constructions marks the capacity for nuanced expression of the complex relationships between actions, agents, and circumstances that constitute the fabric of existence.
