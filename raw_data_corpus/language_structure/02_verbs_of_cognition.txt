VERBS OF COGNITION: A STUDY OF MENTAL PROCESS VERBS IN CONTEXT

CHAPTER I: ON THE NATURE OF COGNITIVE VERBS

Verbs of cognition, those expressions of mental activity whereby the mind engages with its objects, constitute a particularly significant class of vocabulary. Unlike verbs of physical action—"run," "build," "destroy"—cognitive verbs name the processes whereby the mind perceives, understands, reasons, and judges. It is through cognitive verbs that discourse becomes capable of describing its own operations, of reflecting upon the very activities whereby reflection occurs.

The cognitive verbs may be classified into several categories according to the mental operations they express: verbs of perception, verbs of understanding, verbs of reasoning, verbs of judgment, and verbs of intention. Each category merits examination, both for its semantic content and for the syntactic patterns characteristic of its members.

CHAPTER II: VERBS OF PERCEPTION

Verbs of perception name the processes whereby the mind receives impressions from the external world through the senses. Though perception involves the bodily organs—the eye, the ear, the touch—it is fundamentally a mental act, for the raw sensory impression must be interpreted by the mind before it becomes meaningful experience.

SEE, BEHOLD, WITNESS

The verb "see" denotes the most basic visual perception: the reception of visual impressions. Yet "see" extends beyond the literal to the figurative, denoting understanding: "I see what you mean." The verb "behold," more formal and archaic, carries connotations of wonder or significance: "Behold the mystery!" The verb "witness" implies observation of an event, often with legal or moral significance: "She witnessed the accident."

"She saw the figure approaching through the mist, though she could not yet discern its features. As it drew nearer, she beheld with astonishment that it was her long-lost brother, returned from distant lands. She had witnessed many strange events in her life, but none so surprising as this."

HEAR, LISTEN, HEED

The verb "hear" denotes the reception of auditory impressions; "listen" adds the element of attention and intention; "heed" adds the element of taking seriously, of acting upon what is heard.

"He heard the distant sound of bells, faint yet persistent. He stopped to listen more carefully, straining to catch the direction from which they came. His companion had warned him of the danger, but he had not heeded the warning, and now he wondered whether the bells portended the very danger foretold."

PERCEIVE, DISCERN, DISCERN

The verb "perceive" denotes the general act of becoming aware through any sense; "discern" implies the ability to distinguish, to perceive clearly what is obscure or confused; "detect" implies discovery of what is hidden or subtle.

"She perceived a change in his manner, a subtle withdrawal that had not been present before. Looking more closely, she discerned beneath his smile a current of anxiety. She detected in his voice a tremor that belied his apparent confidence."

OBSERVE, NOTICE, REMARK

The verb "observe" implies careful and sustained attention; "notice" implies becoming aware, often suddenly or incidentally; "remark" implies noticing with comment, often with the implication of finding something noteworthy.

"The naturalist observed the behavior of the birds over many weeks, recording their patterns of feeding and nesting. One day he noticed an unusual gathering at a particular tree. He remarked to his companion that such gatherings often preceded the discovery of a rich food source."

CHAPTER III: VERBS OF UNDERSTANDING

Verbs of understanding name the processes whereby the mind grasps meaning, comprehends relations, and achieves insight. These verbs express the transition from confusion to clarity, from ignorance to knowledge.

UNDERSTAND, COMPREHEND, GRASP

The verb "understand" is the most general term, denoting the achievement of mental possession of meaning; "comprehend" implies a more complete and systematic understanding, often of complex matters; "grasp" implies a firm mental hold, often achieved with effort.

"She read the letter three times before she understood its implications. The writer had been deliberately obscure, but careful study enabled her to comprehend the full scope of his meaning. At last she grasped what he had been trying to convey: the secret location of the treasure."

KNOW, APPREHEND, APPRECIATE

The verb "know" denotes the possession of information or understanding; "apprehend" denotes the mental seizing of an idea, often with the implication of perceiving its significance; "appreciate" denotes the recognition of value or significance.

"He knew the facts of the case, having studied the documents carefully. But not until the witness spoke did he apprehend the true nature of the conspiracy. Only then could he appreciate the courage of those who had risked everything to reveal it."

REALIZE, RECOGNIZE, IDENTIFY

The verb "realize" denotes the achievement of awareness, often of something previously overlooked; "recognize" denotes the identification of something as previously known or understood; "identify" denotes the establishment of identity or nature.

"She had walked past the building many times without noticing it. Suddenly she realized that it was the very place described in the old manuscript. She recognized the carved symbol above the door as the one she had seen in the illustration. She identified the location at last: the ancient library, hidden in plain sight."

CHAPTER IV: VERBS OF REASONING

Verbs of reasoning name the processes whereby the mind moves from premises to conclusions, from evidence to inference, from the known to the unknown. These verbs express the logical operations that distinguish rational thought.

THINK, REASON, REFLECT

The verb "think" is the most general term, denoting the exercise of mind upon a subject; "reason" denotes the process of drawing conclusions from premises; "reflect" denotes the turning of thought back upon itself, the examination of one's own ideas or experiences.

"He thought about the problem for many days, turning it over in his mind. He reasoned that if the hypothesis were true, certain consequences must follow. He reflected upon his own assumptions, questioning whether they were justified."

CONSIDER, CONTEMPLATE, PONDER

The verb "consider" denotes the examination of something with attention, typically with a view to decision; "contemplate" denotes sustained and often meditative attention; "ponder" denotes the weighing of something, as if measuring its importance or truth.

"The council considered the proposal from every angle, debating its merits and defects. The philosopher contemplated the nature of existence, seeking understanding beyond the practical. The judge pondered the evidence, seeking the just verdict."

INFER, DEDUCE, CONCLUDE

The verb "infer" denotes the drawing of a conclusion from evidence; "deduce" denotes the drawing of a conclusion from premises by logical necessity; "conclude" denotes the reaching of a final judgment or decision after consideration.

"From the footprints in the sand, she inferred that someone had passed recently. Examining them more closely, she deduced from their depth that the person had been carrying a heavy burden. She concluded that the fugitive had passed this way, heading toward the mountains."

SPECULATE, HYPOTHESIZE, THEORIZE

The verb "speculate" denotes the formation of opinions without sufficient evidence; "hypothesize" denotes the formulation of a proposition to be tested; "theorize" denotes the construction of a systematic explanation.

"The investigators speculated about the motive, each offering a different theory. The scientist hypothesized that the phenomenon resulted from a previously unknown force. The scholar theorized that the text belonged to an earlier period than had been supposed."

CHAPTER V: VERBS OF JUDGMENT

Verbs of judgment name the processes whereby the mind evaluates, assesses, and forms opinions. These verbs express the exercise of critical faculty, the application of standards to the objects of thought.

JUDGE, ASSESS, EVALUATE

The verb "judge" denotes the formation of an opinion or verdict; "assess" denotes the estimation of value, importance, or extent; "evaluate" denotes the systematic examination and judgment.

"The critic judged the work to be of the highest merit. The committee assessed the damage and estimated the cost of repairs. The panel evaluated the proposals according to established criteria, ranking them in order of excellence."

ESTIMATE, CALCATE, COMPUTE

The verb "estimate" denotes the formation of an approximate judgment; "calculate" denotes the determination by mathematical process; "compute" denotes the computation, typically by mechanical or electronic means.

"The architect estimated the time required for the project. The engineer calculated the load the bridge could bear. The analyst computed the probability of success."

CRITICIZE, ANALYZE, INTERPRET

The verb "criticize" denotes the expression of judgment, typically involving both praise and blame; "analyze" denotes the separation into parts for examination; "interpret" denotes the explanation of meaning.

"The reviewer criticized the novel, praising its style but faulting its plot. The linguist analyzed the sentence into its constituent parts. The scholar interpreted the obscure passage, offering a new reading."

CHAPTER VI: VERBS OF INTENTION

Verbs of intention name the processes whereby the mind forms purposes, makes decisions, and directs itself toward future action. These verbs express the volitional aspect of mental life, the will as distinguished from the intellect.

INTEND, PURPOSE, DESIGN

The verb "intend" denotes the having of an aim or plan; "purpose" denotes the setting of an end toward which action is directed; "design" denotes the planning with attention to means and details.

"She intended to visit the museum, but other obligations intervened. He purposed to master the art, devoting himself to practice. The architect designed the building with attention to both beauty and function."

RESOLVE, DETERMINE, DECIDE

The verb "resolve" denotes the settling of a question or the firm adoption of a course; "determine" denotes the fixing of a result or the reaching of a conclusion; "decide" denotes the bringing of uncertainty to an end by choice.

"After much deliberation, she resolved to accept the offer. The investigation determined the cause of the accident. The committee decided in favor of the proposal."

PLAN, DEVISE, CONTRIVE

The verb "plan" denotes the formulation of a method for achieving an end; "devise" denotes the invention of a means; "contrive" denotes the clever or artful design, sometimes with implications of ingenuity or even deception.

"They planned their journey carefully, mapping each stage. The inventor devised a new mechanism for the machine. The strategist contrived a plan to outmaneuver the enemy."

CHAPTER VII: SYNTACTIC PATTERNS OF COGNITIVE VERBS

Cognitive verbs exhibit characteristic syntactic patterns that distinguish them from other verb classes. These patterns reflect the nature of mental processes and their objects.

COGNITIVE VERBS WITH THAT-CLAUSES

Many cognitive verbs take that-clauses as objects, expressing the content of the mental act:

"She knew that the answer was correct."
"He realized that he had made an error."
"They concluded that the hypothesis was false."

The that-clause expresses a proposition, which is the object of the mental act. In some constructions, "that" may be omitted: "She knew the answer was correct."

COGNITIVE VERBS WITH WH-CLAUSES

Cognitive verbs may take wh-clauses (clauses introduced by "what," "who," "where," "when," "why," "how"):

"She wondered what had happened."
"He asked where they were going."
"They discussed how to proceed."

The wh-clause expresses an indirect question, the answer to which is the object of the mental act.

COGNITIVE VERBS WITH INFINITIVES

Certain cognitive verbs take infinitive complements:

"She decided to leave."
"He intended to write."
"They planned to meet."

The infinitive expresses the content of the intention or decision. Other cognitive verbs take infinitives with subjects:

"She expected him to arrive."
"He believed her to be sincere."
"They supposed the task to be easy."

Here the infinitive has its own subject, expressing a proposition about that subject.

COGNITIVE VERBS WITH GERUNDS

Certain cognitive verbs take gerund complements:

"She considered leaving."
"He contemplated retiring."
"They discussed building a new house."

The gerund expresses an action as the object of the mental act.

COGNITIVE VERBS WITH DIRECT AND INDIRECT OBJECTS

Verbs of showing, telling, and teaching may take both direct and indirect objects:

"She showed him the manuscript."
"He told her the secret."
"They taught the students the method."

The indirect object denotes the person who receives the knowledge; the direct object denotes the content conveyed.

Thus do cognitive verbs enable discourse about the very processes whereby discourse is produced and understood. Their mastery marks the capacity for reflection, for thinking about thinking, for the self-conscious examination of mental life.
