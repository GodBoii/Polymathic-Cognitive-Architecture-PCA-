WORD FORMATION AND DERIVATIONS: THE ARCHITECTURE OF THE ENGLISH LEXICON

CHAPTER I: ON THE NATURE OF WORD FORMATION

Word formation, that process whereby new words are created from existing elements, constitutes one of the principal means whereby the lexicon of a language expands and adapts to new expressive needs. English, having absorbed vocabulary from numerous sources throughout its history, possesses a particularly rich system of word formation, combining native Germanic processes with borrowed Latin and Greek elements. Through word formation, the speaker creates new words from old, extending the resources of the language without requiring the borrowing of foreign terms.

The principal processes of word formation in English include derivation, compounding, conversion, clipping, blending, and acronym formation. Each process creates new words according to regular patterns, and each merits examination for the light it sheds upon the structure and flexibility of the English lexicon.

CHAPTER II: DERIVATION BY PREFIXATION

Derivation by prefixation involves the addition of a prefix—an element placed before the base—to create a new word. Prefixes typically modify the meaning of the base without changing its grammatical category.

NEGATIVE AND PRIVATIVE PREFIXES

"Un-" indicates negation or reversal:
"unhappy" (not happy)
"undo" (reverse the doing)
"unusual" (not usual)

"Non-" indicates negation, often with hyphen:
"non-existent"
"non-fiction"
"non-profit"

"In-" (and its variants "im-," "il-," "ir-") indicates negation:
"incomplete" (not complete)
"impossible" (not possible)
"illegible" (not legible)
"irregular" (not regular)

"Dis-" indicates negation or reversal:
"disagree" (not agree)
"disconnect" (reverse the connection)
"dishonest" (not honest)

"A-" indicates negation or absence:
"amoral" (without morality)
"atypical" (not typical)
"asymmetrical" (without symmetry)

"De-" indicates reversal or removal:
"deconstruct" (reverse the construction)
"dehydrate" (remove water)
"defrost" (remove frost)

REVERSATIVE PREFIXES

"Un-" indicates reversal of action:
"unlock" (reverse the locking)
"unfold" (reverse the folding)
"uncover" (reverse the covering)

"Dis-" indicates reversal:
"disconnect" (reverse the connection)
"disassemble" (reverse the assembly)
"disengage" (reverse the engagement)

"De-" indicates reversal:
"decentralize" (reverse the centralization)
"deactivate" (reverse the activation)
"declassify" (reverse the classification)

PEJORATIVE PREFIXES

"Mis-" indicates wrongness or error:
"misunderstand" (understand wrongly)
"misplace" (place wrongly)
"misuse" (use wrongly)

"Mal-" indicates badness or poor quality:
"malfunction" (function poorly)
"malnutrition" (poor nutrition)
"maladjusted" (poorly adjusted)

PREFIXES OF SIZE AND DEGREE

"Super-" indicates exceeding or above:
"supernatural" (above the natural)
"superhuman" (exceeding the human)
"supersede" (sit above)

"Sub-" indicates below or under:
"substandard" (below standard)
"subconscious" (below consciousness)
"submarine" (under the sea)

"Over-" indicates excessive:
"overwork" (work excessively)
"overestimate" (estimate excessively)
"overqualified" (qualified excessively)

"Under-" indicates insufficient:
"underwork" (work insufficiently)
"underestimate" (estimate insufficiently)
"underqualified" (qualified insufficiently)

"Mini-" indicates small:
"miniskirt" (small skirt)
"minibus" (small bus)
"minimal" (smallest)

"Micro-" indicates very small:
"microscope" (instrument for seeing very small things)
"microorganism" (very small organism)
"microcosm" (small world)

"Macro-" indicates large:
"macrocosm" (large world)
"macroeconomics" (economics of large systems)
"macrostructure" (large structure)

PREFIXES OF ATTITUDE

"Co-" indicates together or jointly:
"cooperate" (operate together)
"coauthor" (author jointly)
"coexist" (exist together)

"Counter-" indicates against or opposite:
"counteract" (act against)
"counterargument" (argument against)
"counterclockwise" (opposite to clockwise)

"Anti-" indicates against:
"antisocial" (against society)
"antibiotic" (against life, specifically bacteria)
"antidote" (against poison)

"Pro-" indicates for or in favor of:
"pro-democracy" (in favor of democracy)
"proactive" (acting in advance)
"proponent" (one who argues for)

LOCATIVE PREFIXES

"Super-" indicates above:
"superstructure" (structure above)
"supernatural" (above nature)

"Sub-" indicates under:
"subway" (way under)
"subsoil" (soil under)
"subtitle" (title under)

"Inter-" indicates between:
"international" (between nations)
"interact" (act between)
"intermediate" (between middle)

"Trans-" indicates across:
"transport" (carry across)
"translate" (carry across languages)
"transform" (change form across)

"Circum-" indicates around:
"circumnavigate" (sail around)
"circumscribe" (write around)
"circumstance" (standing around)

PREFIXES OF TIME AND ORDER

"Pre-" indicates before:
"prehistoric" (before history)
"predict" (say before)
"prevent" (come before)

"Post-" indicates after:
"postwar" (after war)
"postpone" (place after)
"postscript" (written after)

"Ex-" indicates former:
"ex-president" (former president)
"ex-wife" (former wife)
"ex-student" (former student)

"Re-" indicates again:
"rewrite" (write again)
"reconsider" (consider again)
"rebuild" (build again)

"Retro-" indicates backward:
"retrospect" (look backward)
"retrograde" (going backward)
"retroactive" (acting backward)

NUMERICAL PREFIXES

"Uni-" indicates one:
"unilateral" (one-sided)
"uniform" (one form)
"unique" (one of a kind)

"Bi-" indicates two:
"bilateral" (two-sided)
"bilingual" (two languages)
"biannual" (twice a year)

"Tri-" indicates three:
"trilateral" (three-sided)
"trilingual" (three languages)
"triangle" (three angles)

"Multi-" indicates many:
"multilateral" (many-sided)
"multilingual" (many languages)
"multifaceted" (many facets)

"Poly-" indicates many:
"polygon" (many angles)
"polysyllabic" (many syllables)
"polyglot" (many languages)

OTHER COMMON PREFIXES

"Auto-" indicates self:
"autobiography" (self-life-writing)
"automatic" (self-moving)
"autonomous" (self-governing)

"Neo-" indicates new:
"neoclassical" (new classical)
"neologism" (new word)
"neonatal" (newly born)

CHAPTER III: DERIVATION BY SUFFIXATION

Derivation by suffixation involves the addition of a suffix—an element placed after the base—to create a new word. Unlike prefixes, suffixes often change the grammatical category of the base.

NOUN-FORMING SUFFIXES

From verbs:
"-er" / "-or": "teacher," "actor" (one who does)
"-tion" / "-sion": "creation," "decision" (act or result)
"-ment": "development," "achievement" (result or state)
"-al": "refusal," "arrival" (act or instance)
"-ance" / "-ence": "performance," "existence" (act or quality)
"-ing": "building," "meeting" (act or result)

From adjectives:
"-ness": "happiness," "darkness" (quality or state)
"-ity" / "-ty": "reality," "safety" (quality or state)
"-dom": "freedom," "wisdom" (state or condition)

From nouns:
"-hood": "childhood," "neighborhood" (state or condition)
"-ship": "friendship," "leadership" (state or condition)
"-dom": "kingdom," "dukedom" (state or domain)

ADJECTIVE-FORMING SUFFIXES

From nouns:
"-al" / "-ial": "cultural," "financial" (pertaining to)
"-ic" / "-ical": "historic," "historical" (pertaining to)
"-ous" / "-ious": "famous," "delicious" (having the quality of)
"-ful": "beautiful," "helpful" (full of)
"-less": "hopeless," "careless" (without)
"-ly": "friendly," "daily" (having the quality of)
"-en": "golden," "wooden" (made of)
"-ish": "childish," "foolish" (having the quality of)
"-like": "childlike," "dreamlike" (resembling)
"-y": "rainy," "windy" (characterized by)

From verbs:
"-able" / "-ible": "readable," "visible" (able to be)
"-ive": "creative," "active" (having the tendency to)
"-ent" / "-ant": "different," "important" (having the quality of)
"-ory": "contradictory," "satisfactory" (having the quality of)

VERB-FORMING SUFFIXES

From nouns and adjectives:
"-ize" / "-ise": "organize," "realize" (make or become)
"-ify" / "-fy": "simplify," "beautify" (make or become)
"-en": "strengthen," "widen" (make or become)
"-ate": "activate," "motivate" (make or become)

ADVERB-FORMING SUFFIXES

From adjectives:
"-ly": "quickly," "carefully" (in a manner)
"-ward" / "-wards": "backward," "forwards" (in a direction)
"-wise": "clockwise," "otherwise" (in a manner or respect)

CHAPTER IV: COMPOUNDING

Compounding involves the combination of two or more bases to form a single word. English makes extensive use of compounding, creating words that express complex concepts with economy and precision.

COMPOUND NOUNS

Noun + noun:
"bookshelf" (shelf for books)
"manuscript" (script written by hand)
"sunlight" (light from the sun)

Adjective + noun:
"blackboard" (board that is black)
"greenhouse" (house that is green)
"blueprint" (print that is blue)

Verb + noun:
"pickpocket" (one who picks pockets)
"breakfast" (fast that one breaks)
"driveway" (way for driving)

Noun + verb:
"rainfall" (fall of rain)
"haircut" (cut of hair)
"sunset" (setting of sun)

Preposition + noun:
"underground" (ground under)
"overcoat" (coat over)
"aftermath" (math after)

COMPOUND ADJECTIVES

Adjective + noun:
"blue-eyed" (having blue eyes)
"long-term" (for a long term)
"old-fashioned" (in an old fashion)

Adjective + adjective:
"bittersweet" (bitter and sweet)
"red-hot" (red and hot)
"icy-cold" (icy and cold)

Noun + adjective:
"seasick" (sick from the sea)
"homeless" (without a home)
"worldwide" (wide as the world)

Noun + participle:
"man-made" (made by man)
"sun-dried" (dried by the sun)
"weather-beaten" (beaten by weather)

Adjective + participle:
"ready-made" (made ready)
"new-born" (born new)
"old-established" (established old)

Adverb + participle:
"well-known" (known well)
"everlasting" (lasting ever)
"never-ending" (ending never)

COMPOUND VERBS

Noun + verb:
"to baby-sit" (to sit with a baby)
"to sleepwalk" (to walk while sleeping)
"to daydream" (to dream during the day)

Adjective + verb:
"to whitewash" (to wash white)
"to safeguard" (to guard safe)
"to dry-clean" (to clean dry)

Preposition + verb:
"to underestimate" (to estimate under)
"to outdo" (to do beyond)
"to overtake" (to take over)

CHAPTER V: CONVERSION

Conversion, also termed zero-derivation, involves the creation of a new word from an existing word without any change in form. The new word belongs to a different grammatical category from the original.

NOUN TO VERB CONVERSION

"to table" (from "table," a piece of furniture)
"to chair" (from "chair," a piece of furniture)
"to book" (from "book," a volume)
"to mail" (from "mail," post)
"to telephone" (from "telephone," an instrument)
"to bottle" (from "bottle," a container)
"to can" (from "can," a container)
"to butter" (from "butter," a food)
"to water" (from "water," a liquid)

VERB TO NOUN CONVERSION

"a call" (from "to call")
"a run" (from "to run")
"a walk" (from "to walk")
"a look" (from "to look")
"a smile" (from "to smile")
"a laugh" (from "to laugh")
"a doubt" (from "to doubt")
"a guess" (from "to guess")

ADJECTIVE TO NOUN CONVERSION

"the poor" (from "poor")
"the rich" (from "rich")
"the old" (from "old")
"the young" (from "young")
"the blind" (from "blind")
"the deaf" (from "deaf")

ADJECTIVE TO VERB CONVERSION

"to empty" (from "empty")
"to dry" (from "dry")
"to clean" (from "clean")
"to dirty" (from "dirty")
"to slow" (from "slow")
"to warm" (from "warm")
"to cool" (from "cool")

CHAPTER VI: OTHER PROCESSES OF WORD FORMATION

CLIPPING

Clipping involves the shortening of a word without changing its meaning or grammatical category.

Back clipping (removing the end):
"exam" (from "examination")
"gym" (from "gymnasium")
"lab" (from "laboratory")
"math" (from "mathematics")
"phone" (from "telephone")
"photo" (from "photograph")

Front clipping (removing the beginning):
"bus" (from "omnibus")
"plane" (from "airplane")
"phone" (from "telephone")

Middle clipping:
"flu" (from "influenza")
"fridge" (from "refrigerator")

BLENDING

Blending involves the combination of parts of two words to form a new word.

"brunch" (breakfast + lunch)
"smog" (smoke + fog)
"motel" (motor + hotel)
"telecast" (television + broadcast)
"infotainment" (information + entertainment)
"edutainment" (education + entertainment)

ACRONYM FORMATION

Acronyms are words formed from the initial letters of a name or phrase.

Pronounced as words:
"NASA" (National Aeronautics and Space Administration)
"laser" (Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation)
"radar" (Radio Detection And Ranging)
"scuba" (Self-Contained Underwater Breathing Apparatus)

Pronounced as sequences of letters:
"FBI" (Federal Bureau of Investigation)
"CIA" (Central Intelligence Agency)
"PhD" (Doctor of Philosophy)
"TV" (television)

INITIALISM

Initialisms are similar to acronyms but are always pronounced as sequences of letters:
"USA" (United States of America)
"UK" (United Kingdom)
"UFO" (Unidentified Flying Object)

BACK-FORMATION

Back-formation involves the creation of a word by removing a supposed affix from an existing word.

"to edit" (from "editor," by removing "-or")
"to televise" (from "television," by removing "-ion")
"to donate" (from "donation," by removing "-ion")
"to babysit" (from "babysitter," by removing "-er")
"to burgle" (from "burglar," by removing "-ar")

REDUPLICATION

Reduplication involves the repetition of a word or part of a word.

Exact reduplication:
"hush-hush"
"go-go"
"no-no"

Rhyming reduplication:
"hocus-pocus"
"helter-skelter"
"nitty-gritty"

Ablaut reduplication (vowel change):
"chit-chat"
"ding-dong"
"zig-zag"
"ping-pong"

Thus through the various processes of word formation does the English lexicon expand and adapt, creating new words from existing elements according to regular patterns. The mastery of these processes marks the capacity for understanding the structure of the vocabulary and for extending it to meet new expressive needs.
