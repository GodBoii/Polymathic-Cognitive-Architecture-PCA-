Not a fragment, but the whole: Map-based evaluation
of data-driven Fire Danger Index models
Shahbaz Alvi1*, Jose Maria Costa Saura1, and Italo Epicoco1,2
1CMCC Foundation - Euro-Mediterranean Center on Climate Change, Italy
2Department of Engineering for Innovation, University of Salento, Italy
*Corresponding author: shahbaz.alvi@cmcc.it
Abstract
A growing body of literature has focused on predicting wildfire occurrence using
machine learning methods, capitalizing on high-resolution data and fire predictors
that canonical process-based frameworks largely ignore. Standard evaluation met-
rics for an ML classifier, while important, provide a potentially limited measure of
the model’s operational performance for the Fire Danger Index (FDI) forecast. Fur-
thermore, model evaluation is frequently conducted without adequately accounting
for false positive rates, despite their critical relevance in operational contexts. In
this paper, we revisit the daily FDI model evaluation paradigm and propose a novel
method for evaluating a forest fire forecasting model that is aligned with real-world
decision-making. Furthermore, we systematically assess performance in accurately
predicting fire activity and the false positives (false alarms). We further demonstrate
that an ensemble of ML models improves both fire identification and reduces false
positives.
Keywords: daily fire danger index, data-driven fire danger index, wildfires, model
performance, machine learning model ensemble, daily FDI.
1.
Introduction
Wildfires are becoming an increasing concern under climate change, as rising temper-
atures, prolonged droughts, and more frequent heat extremes intensify fire weather and
extend fire seasons across many regions [1]. This escalation in fire activity places growing
pressure on operational wildfire management, with direct implications for the effectiveness
of prevention, preparedness, and suppression strategies. In this context, improving early
warning systems has become a critical priority for reducing impacts on lives, infrastruc-
ture, and essential services under current and future climate conditions.

Early warning systems rely on forecasting the danger associated with wildfire occur-
rence. Fire danger forecast is typically represented by a numeric indicator encoding the
danger associated with a forest fire (ease of ignition, rate of spread, etc.) [2]. The in-
dex so computed is mapped to danger classes based on the wildfire history of the target
geographic region and input from wildfire risk assessment experts. For many decades,
process-based methods have been used to assess the wildfire danger.
These methods
primarily exploit meteorological variables to assess the wildfire danger on a given day.
However, wildfires result from a complex interaction between many variables, of which
weather variables are a subset. In fact, the probability of a forest fire on a given day is
a joint probability of fire predictors (weather, vegetation, human presence, etc.) being in
the favorable range. Furthermore, in the current Anthropocene era, forest fires primarily
result from human activity [3]. Thus, including only a subset of the actual fire drivers
can systematically overestimate the danger of forest fire occurrence [4].
The recent surge in artificial intelligence and machine learning research has accelerated
the development of wildfire prediction models (see [5] for a review), complemented by
the growing availability of spatial data [6, 7]. These approaches demonstrate improved
performance with respect to canonical methods based solely on meteorological variables,
such as the Canadian Fire Weather Index [4]. The first attempts at using ML techniques
utilized architectures like Random Forest (RF)
[8–10] and Artificial Neural Networks
(ANN) [11–13] for forecasting wildfire activity. More recently, architectures that enable
deep learning, such as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) [14], have been adopted
across various wildfire-related applications. The vanilla CNN can extract local surface
features and the spatial distribution. In contrast, the ConvLSTM architecture integrates
spatial imagery with temporal dynamics, which are especially important as different fire
drivers influence fire occurrence on different time scales. Among the sizable body of work
in which the CNN and ConvLSTM have been studied for the use case of fire occurrence,
some works worth mentioning are [15–23].
Applications of ML in forecasting fire activity rely on standard machine learning evalu-
ation metrics, such as Recall, Precision or ROC, to quantify model performance. However,
conventional performance evaluation of an ML model largely reflects its performance un-
der controlled conditions (e.g., on a balanced dataset), which may not fully capture the
real-world, large-scale operational scenario. For instance, metrics computed on a dedic-
ated test dataset that is, at least partially, similar to the training dataset, systematically
bias the metrics and create a false sense of accuracy in forecasting daily fire activity.
Moreover, model evaluations that go beyond standard metrics predominantly prioritize
successful fire event prediction and systematically undervalue the equally important task
of reducing false positives. Indeed, the question of false positives extends beyond a purely
statistical concern and encompasses substantial economic costs arising from unnecessary
resource mobilization. Repeated false alerts lead to alert fatigue among practitioners, pro-
gressively eroding trust in early warning systems and even undermining their adoption
2
by civil protection and first responders. Thus, a model with good predictive performance
but a high false-alarm rate is still of limited practical value.
In this manuscript, we propose a novel model evaluation that complements the ca-
nonical approach. Our work fills the existing gap between ML model evaluation on the
test dataset and its actual field performance. This validation is indeed crucial before ML
models are integrated into the existing firefighting operational chain. We further apply
our approach to visualize and systematically compare model performance in flagging false
positives (false alarms), going beyond qualitative comparisons that are largely absent from
the existing literature. In Section 2, we explain the architecture of the neural networks
considered in this work, the dataset and the target geographic region, and our sampling
strategy and training methodology. In Section 3, we present the results of our analysis.
In Sections 4 and 5, we discuss our findings and present our conclusions, respectively.
2.
Methodology
The CNN [14] and ConvLSTM [24] architectures have been explored for forecasting FDI
at global [15] and regional scale [19]. CNNs can capture complex nonlinear relationships
in heterogeneous data, such as the interplay between topography, vegetation, and human
activities for fire occurrence.
The proposed method frames fire detection as a patch-
based binary classification problem.
The input image is partitioned into overlapping
tiles, each centered on a target pixel and assigned a binary label (fire / no-fire). The
CNN is trained on this labeled tile dataset. However, a vanilla CNN does not account
for temporal correlation. Therefore, it may be difficult to address the question, “how
do the weather conditions yesterday or n days before yesterday affect the probability
of fires today?”.
Several studies report improved performance with a ConvLSTM, an
amalgamation of CNN and LSTM architectures [15, 17, 19]. The hybrid architecture
of ConvLSTM efficiently extracts spatiotemporal correlations, which are important for
forecasting fire occurrence.
Indeed, fire predictors vary on different time scales, e.g.,
weather on a daily scale, vegetation typically on a 10-day scale, and constant topological
variables. At inference time, the network is applied to all tiles extracted from a test image,
and the resulting class probabilities are mapped back to their corresponding center pixels,
yielding a segmented image corresponding to a dense probability map in which each pixel
encodes the likelihood of fire.
2.1
Architecture
In this work, we compare the performance of three variations of the vanilla CNN archi-
tecture and a ConvLSTM. The architecture and the model training are set up in the
PyTorch Lightning framework [25], and MLFlow is utilized for tracking model training
and logging. The PyTorch ReduceLROnPlateau is used as the learning rate scheduler and
the ADAM optimizer [26], which uses estimates of the first and second moments of the
3
gradients to compute adaptive learning rates for each parameter. The network’s output
is the log of the normalized probabilities from LogSoftmax layer, corresponding to the
sample’s assignment to the “fire” and “no-fire” label. Furthermore, we have utilized the
negative log-likelihood as the loss function.
We first consider a vanilla CNN (henceforth called the Basic CNN ) composed of a
convolution block consisting of a single 2D Convolution layer with a kernel size of 3
and hidden layer size 16, followed by 2D BatchNorm, a ReLu, and a MaxPool2D layer. The
flattened output is passed to the classifier block with three fully connected layers, in which
each layer is followed by a BatchNorm, ReLU, and Dropout layer. The total number of
trainable parameters in the Basic CNN sums to 40.6K. The Basic CNN is an efficient image
classifier (i.e., it classifies image patches) but lacks the depth to correlate more subtle
surface features across multiple fire predictors. We will see later that this is important for
improving the false positive rate. To compare the Basic CNN with deeper networks, we
consider two variants of the CNN. We consider a second CNN with 3 convolution blocks
and a single classifier block composed of three fully connected layers (henceforth referred
to as Deeper CNN 1), consisting of a total of 66.5K learnable parameters. The third
CNN variation has 4 Convolution blocks and a single classifier (henceforth referred to as
Deeper CNN 2), with a total of 111K learnable parameters. The ConvLSTM architecture
used in this work is taken from [19] and is utilized without additional modifications. The
network consists of a single ConvLSTMCell (which includes an LSTM block), followed by
a convolution block and a classifier block. The total number of learnable parameters is
371K.
2.2
Dataset and case study
The training and validation samples are derived from the publicly available dataset pub-
lished in [19]. The high-resolution data cube [27] is geographically centered on Greece and
includes the Balkan peninsula and western Turkey, covering a total geographical footprint
of 1,253 km × 983 km at a spatial resolution of 1 km and a daily temporal resolution.
Figure 1 shows a map of the target region.
Many fire drivers in the dataset are first-order statistics of hourly measurements, such
as the daily minimum/maximum. Variables originally acquired on an n-daily scale are
downscaled to daily resolution.
Static variables do not possess a temporal dimension
(for more information regarding the data, see [27]). The comprehensive dataset spans
the years 2009 to 2021 and includes 90 fire drivers derived from ERA-5 Land weather
data, MODIS vegetation indices, soil moisture, and human-related factors like population
density. The target variable, the burn mask of a given day, is derived from historical
burned area records (> 30 ha) from the European Forest Fire Information System, which
are intersected with MODIS active fire data to recover the start date of each fire event.
Additionally, the dataset also contains the Corine Land Cover (CLC) classes of the target
region. The CLC assigns each pixel on the map one of the 44 categories [28]. The CLC

2.3
Sampling strategy and training setup
2.3.1
Sampling strategy
Each sample is a multi-channel 25 × 25 “image” centered on the pixel labeled as “fire”
(0) or “no-fire” (1). The model is trained to classify the images as “fire” or “no-fire”. This
training approach is interesting from an operational standpoint for two reasons. First,
the model learns to associate fire occurrence with local-area information (e.g., vegetation,
micro-climatic effects), which is crucial for operational forecasting.
Second, with this
approach, we can conveniently build the training dataset, balancing the ratio of negative
to positive samples. The strategy for sampling the fire and no-fire samples is crucial in
this approach and has a direct impact on the skill of the classifier. Unfortunately, there
is no clear recipe for curating no-fire samples from the large pool of available samples,
and various strategies have been adopted with some similarities (see [16, 19, 20]). The
sampling strategy not only affects model performance on the dedicated test dataset but
also profoundly affects the model’s operational performance.

There are 25,605 fire samples in the dataset, and more than 250,000 no-fire samples.
However, the conditions associated with the two scenarios partially overlap, given the
stochastic nature of fire occurrence. We built the training dataset, balancing the similar-
ities and differences between the fire and no-fire samples. Experimenting with different
sampling strategies revealed that no-fire samples closely resembling fire samples led to a
systematic overestimation of fire activity. While such a model could, in practice, fore-
cast all fire instances with high certainty, such forecasts are not operationally meaningful.
In fact, such a model cannot support differential analysis between regions with varying
fire danger. The strategy adopted in this work for sampling no-fire samples is primarily
driven by considerations of the model’s operational performance in predicting the daily
FDI. The model evaluation approach adopted in this work focuses not only on successful
fire detection on a given day but also on the number of false positives flagged by the model
during full-map model inference. After several iterations in which different strategies were
considered, the following strategy was adopted:
1. The number of no-fire samples is twice the number of fire samples in a given year.
This balances the number of positive and negative samples presented to the model
7
during the training phase and ensures a sufficiently large distribution of no-fire
samples in the feature space.
2. Within each year, the no-fire samples are chosen from the period between the first
and the last fire detection date (the fire season).
3. Within the fire season, no-fire samples are drawn on the dates when no fires were
recorded in the entire region of interest.
4. No-fire samples are drawn from locations where the dominant land cover type is
theoretically susceptible to fires, even if no fires have been registered on that land
cover in the dataset. This spans the land cover labels 12 to 32 (inclusive).
5. Samples are curated so that no negative sample has the same location as a positive
sample in a given year.
6. All negative samples have unique locations in a given year.
We drew 23,742 fire samples and 47,484 no-fire samples, which are split chronologically:
samples from 2009 to 2018 are used for training, validation samples from 2019, and samples
from 2020 and 2021 are reserved for testing. The model is trained for 150 epochs. Epoch-
averaged loss is used to stabilize the loss curve against rapid fluctuations caused by the
non-uniform distribution of positive and negative samples within batches. During the
training, the epoch-averaged validation loss is monitored, and the network parameters
corresponding to the lowest validation loss are retained as the best model.
We log Recall, Precision, and the F1-score from the training, validation, and test
phases. Indeed, these scores are important for monitoring training progress and identifying
potential issues in the code implementation. Furthermore, they provide valuable insights
into the model behavior and test the sampling strategy for dataset curation.

Results
3.1
Standard ML metrics
In Table 2, we report the Recall, Precision, and F1-score computed on the test dataset
using the model configuration corresponding to the lowest epoch-averaged validation loss.
These metrics are widely adopted in the machine-learning community for assessing clas-
sifier performance. Based on the F1-score reported in the Table 2, one can deduce that
all models perform equally well, showing a good balance in correctly recognizing the fire
and no-fire samples in the test dataset. Based on the recall, ConvLSTM performs slightly
worse than the CNN architectures. A similar trend can also be noted for precision, where
CNN networks perform better than ConvLSTM. Another interesting aspect of the result
is that increasing the complexity of the CNN (from Basic CNN to Deeper CNN 1 to
Deeper CNN 2), there is no significant improvement or degradation in the recall.
3.2
Inference map-based model evaluation
Daily FDI maps play a crucial role in operational wildfire management by forecasting
fire risk.
Therefore, validating the ML model for forecasting fire activity should also
be systematically validated against daily FDI maps. This ensures consistency with the
decision-making framework where the model will ultimately be deployed. Furthermore,
the performance metrics computed on the test dataset, while insightful for model training,
are of limited scope operationally. Indeed, the inference performed on the test dataset
is fundamentally different than the full-map inference performed in the daily operational
scenario. The performance metrics derived from the test dataset represent model per-
formance under controlled conditions, in particular, in which the samples are balanced
(between fire and no-fire classes) or otherwise curated. Indeed, in full-map inference, the
model encounters a wider distribution of samples. Therefore, the model’s performance
evaluated on a test dataset might not accurately reflect its behavior on full-map inference.
Therefore, model evaluation based on full-map inference is a more accurate measure of
model performance as it is more operationally significant.

4.1
Best architecture for forecasting FDI

percentiles reported in Table 3, three conclusions can be drawn. First, all models detect
fewer than 75% of fires on 40% of the days. This result suggests that CNN-based networks

Third, the network
complexity of the CNN architecture does not improve the daily recall, and this result is

the daily fire activity.

5.
Conclusions
This study presents a comprehensive evaluation of data-driven models for forecasting
daily FDI using various CNN architectures. We have also considered a CNN with an
LSTM component, so-called ConvLSTM. The geographic region is centered on a high-
resolution 1 km × 1 km daily data cube covering Greece, the Balkan peninsula, and
western Turkey, curated and furnished by [27]. This work demonstrates the impact of the
neural network models and evaluation strategy on a model’s viability in the operational
chain for forecasting fire activity.
The work proposes a novel approach for evaluation, focusing on its operational per-
formance.
In particular, we emphasize that while standard machine learning metrics
are useful for monitoring the training phase, full-map inference is a more comprehens-
ive measure of a model’s operational viability for daily FDI forecast. This conclusion
is derived from the premise that the test scores can potentially be biased due to the
overlapping samples in the feature space. While standard metrics, such as Recall and
16
Precision, suggest that all neural networks performed comparably, with CNNs showing
slightly higher precision in some tests, full-map inference revealed that ConvLSTM had
high recall on most days. More explicitly, the ConvLSTM effectively handles spatiotem-
poral correlations, integrating rapid meteorological changes with slow-moving vegetation
trends. Operationally, this allowed the ConvLSTM to achieve perfect detection on 40%
of the days, outperforming the models based on pure CNN.
Systematic assessment of false positives flagged by the model is rarely explored in the
literature. Our analysis goes beyond the standard ML paradigm in this aspect. ConvL-
STM has a decisive advantage over the model based on CNN, as it generally positively
skews FDI distribution, indicating fewer false positives. The study further reveals that
increasing complexity, from a Basic CNN to Deeper CNN 2, did not favor daily recall.
However, Deeper CNN 1 was identified as having the optimal depth for confidently flag-
ging no-fire regions. This suggests that simple layers might be sufficient for identifying
a fire event, but capturing subtle correlations for “safe” conditions requires a more nu-
anced architecture. This suggests an interesting line of inquiry for future explorations in
ConvLSTM for forecasting fire activity.
The research also explores an ensemble approach for a more robust FDI forecast.
By averaging over a 7-member ensemble model, a robust estimator was developed. The
ensemble-averaged FDI maps were shown to be more effective at rejecting false positives,
indicating a more confident and less noisy predictor of fire-free areas while maintaining a
robust recall.
Future work in this direction should focus on full-map-based evaluation as the primary
method of model evaluation for both fire detection and false alarm rates.
