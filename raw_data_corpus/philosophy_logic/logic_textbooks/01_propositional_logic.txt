# Chapter 1: Propositional Logic — The Foundations of Formal Reasoning

## 1.1 Introduction to Logical Form

Propositional logic, also known as sentential logic, constitutes the most fundamental branch of formal logic. It deals with the logical relationships between propositions—statements that are either true or false—and the connectives that combine them. This chapter develops the formal apparatus necessary for analyzing arguments, determining validity, and understanding the structure of deductive inference.

The central insight of propositional logic is that the truth value of complex propositions depends systematically on the truth values of their component propositions and the logical connectives that combine them. This systematic dependence enables precise analysis of argument forms.

## 1.2 The Language of Propositional Logic

### 1.2.1 Propositional Variables

The primitive elements of propositional logic are propositional variables, typically denoted by lowercase letters such as p, q, r, s, and so forth. Each propositional variable represents an atomic proposition—a statement that is either true or false, but whose internal structure is not analyzed within the propositional calculus.

The domain of truth values consists of exactly two elements: T (true) and F (false). Every atomic proposition receives exactly one of these two values.

### 1.2.2 Logical Connectives

The following connectives form the standard vocabulary of propositional logic:

**Negation (¬):** The negation of a proposition p is true precisely when p is false. Symbolically: ¬p is true iff p is false.

**Conjunction (∧):** The conjunction of propositions p and q is true precisely when both p and q are true. Symbolically: p ∧ q is true iff p = T and q = T.

**Disjunction (∨):** The disjunction of propositions p and q is true precisely when at least one of p or q is true. Symbolically: p ∨ q is true iff p = T or q = T (inclusive OR).

**Conditional (→):** The conditional "if p then q" is false precisely when p is true and q is false; otherwise it is true. Symbolically: p → q is false iff p = T and q = F. In this construction, p is called the antecedent and q is called the consequent.

**Biconditional (↔):** The biconditional "p if and only if q" is true precisely when p and q have the same truth value. Symbolically: p ↔ q is true iff p = q.

### 1.2.3 Well-Formed Formulas

A well-formed formula (wff) of propositional logic is defined recursively:

1. Any propositional variable is a wff.
2. If α is a wff, then ¬α is a wff.
3. If α and β are wffs, then (α ∧ β), (α ∨ β), (α → β), and (α ↔ β) are wffs.
4. Nothing else is a wff.

Parentheses are used to disambiguate the structure of complex formulas. By convention, negation binds most tightly, followed by conjunction and disjunction, then conditional, then biconditional.

## 1.3 Truth Tables

### 1.3.1 The Fundamental Truth Tables

The truth tables define the meaning of each connective precisely:

| p | q | ¬p | p ∧ q | p ∨ q | p → q | p ↔ q |
|---|---|-----|-------|-------|-------|-------|
| T | T | F   | T     | T     | T     | T     |
| T | F | F   | F     | T     | F     | F     |
| F | T | T   | F     | T     | T     | F     |
| F | F | T   | F     | F     | T     | T     |

### 1.3.2 Truth Table Analysis

Truth tables provide a systematic method for evaluating any propositional formula. To construct a truth table for a formula with n distinct propositional variables:

1. List all 2ⁿ possible combinations of truth values.
2. Compute the truth value of each subformula, working from the simplest components to the most complex.
3. The final column shows the truth value of the complete formula for each possible assignment.

**Example 1.3.1:** Construct the truth table for the formula (p → q) ↔ (¬p ∨ q).

| p | q | p → q | ¬p | ¬p ∨ q | (p → q) ↔ (¬p ∨ q) |
|---|---|-------|-----|--------|---------------------|
| T | T | T     | F  | T      | T                   |
| T | F | F     | F  | F      | T                   |
| F | T | T     | T  | T      | T                   |
| F | F | T     | T  | T      | T                   |

The formula is true under all possible truth value assignments—it is a tautology.

## 1.4 Tautologies, Contradictions, and Contingencies

### 1.4.1 Definitions

A **tautology** is a formula that is true under every possible interpretation. Its truth table column contains only T values. Examples include:
- p ∨ ¬p (Law of Excluded Middle)
- ¬(p ∧ ¬p) (Law of Non-Contradiction)
- (p → q) ↔ (¬p ∨ q) (Material Implication Equivalence)

A **contradiction** (or self-contradiction) is a formula that is false under every possible interpretation. Its truth table column contains only F values. Examples include:
- p ∧ ¬p
- ¬(p ∨ ¬p)

A **contingency** is a formula that is neither a tautology nor a contradiction—its truth value depends on the particular interpretation.

### 1.4.2 The Importance of Tautologies

Tautologies represent logically valid formula patterns. They are the theorems of propositional logic—formulas that hold universally, regardless of what the atomic propositions represent. Any argument whose logical form corresponds to a tautology is valid, regardless of the particular content of the premises.

## 1.5 Logical Equivalence

### 1.5.1 Definition

Two formulas α and β are logically equivalent, written α ≡ β, if and only if they have the same truth value under every possible interpretation. Equivalently, α ↔ β is a tautology.

### 1.5.2 Fundamental Equivalences

The following equivalences are fundamental to logical manipulation:

**Double Negation:** ¬¬p ≡ p

**Commutativity:**
- p ∧ q ≡ q ∧ p
- p ∨ q ≡ q ∨ p
- p ↔ q ≡ q ↔ p

**Associativity:**
- (p ∧ q) ∧ r ≡ p ∧ (q ∧ r)
- (p ∨ q) ∨ r ≡ p ∨ (q ∨ r)

**Distributivity:**
- p ∧ (q ∨ r) ≡ (p ∧ q) ∨ (p ∧ r)
- p ∨ (q ∧ r) ≡ (p ∨ q) ∧ (p ∨ r)

**De Morgan's Laws:**
- ¬(p ∧ q) ≡ ¬p ∨ ¬q
- ¬(p ∨ q) ≡ ¬p ∧ ¬q

**Material Implication:**
- p → q ≡ ¬p ∨ q
- p → q ≡ ¬(p ∧ ¬q)

**Absorption:**
- p ∧ (p ∨ q) ≡ p
- p ∨ (p ∧ q) ≡ p

## 1.6 Logical Consequence and Validity

### 1.6.1 Argument Form

An argument in propositional logic consists of a set of premises Γ = {P₁, P₂, ..., Pₙ} and a conclusion C. The argument is valid if and only if the truth of all premises guarantees the truth of the conclusion—that is, if there is no possible interpretation in which all premises are true and the conclusion is false.

Formally: Γ ⊨ C (the premises logically entail the conclusion) if and only if there is no interpretation I such that I(P₁) = I(P₂) = ... = I(Pₙ) = T and I(C) = F.

### 1.6.2 Validity and Truth Tables

To test an argument for validity using truth tables:

1. Identify all atomic propositions appearing in the argument.
2. Construct a truth table with columns for all premises and the conclusion.
3. Examine every row in which all premises are true.
4. If the conclusion is true in every such row, the argument is valid.
5. If there exists any row where all premises are true but the conclusion is false, the argument is invalid (this is a counterexample).

**Example 1.6.1:** Test the validity of the following argument form:

Premise 1: p → q
Premise 2: p
Conclusion: q

| p | q | p → q | All Premises True? | Conclusion (q) |
|---|---|-------|---------------------|----------------|
| T | T | T     | Yes                | T              |
| T | F | F     | No                 | F              |
| F | T | T     | No                 | T              |
| F | F | T     | No                 | F              |

Only the first row has all premises true, and in that row the conclusion is true. Therefore, the argument is valid. This is the form known as Modus Ponens.

### 1.6.3 Common Valid Argument Forms

**Modus Ponens (Affirming the Antecedent):**
- p → q
- p
∴ q

**Modus Tollens (Denying the Consequent):**
- p → q
- ¬q
∴ ¬p

**Hypothetical Syllogism:**
- p → q
- q → r
∴ p → r

**Disjunctive Syllogism:**
- p ∨ q
- ¬p
∴ q

**Constructive Dilemma:**
- p → r
- q → r
- p ∨ q
∴ r

### 1.6.4 Common Invalid Forms

**Affirming the Consequent:**
- p → q
- q
∴ p
(This form is INVALID. Counterexample: p = F, q = T)

**Denying the Antecedent:**
- p → q
- ¬p
∴ ¬q
(This form is INVALID. Counterexample: p = F, q = F)

## 1.7 Formal Proof Methods

### 1.7.1 Natural Deduction

Natural deduction provides a system of inference rules that allow the construction of formal proofs. Each rule specifies how one formula may be derived from others.

**Conjunction Introduction:** From α and β, infer α ∧ β.

**Conjunction Elimination:** From α ∧ β, infer α (or β).

**Disjunction Introduction:** From α, infer α ∨ β.

**Disjunction Elimination:** From α ∨ β, α → γ, and β → γ, infer γ.

**Negation Introduction:** From assuming α leads to a contradiction, infer ¬α.

**Negation Elimination:** From ¬α leads to a contradiction, infer α.

**Conditional Introduction:** From assuming α leads to β, infer α → β.

**Conditional Elimination:** From α → β and α, infer β.

### 1.7.2 Proof Example

Prove: (p → q) ∧ (q → r) ⊢ p → r

```
1. (p → q) ∧ (q → r)      Premise
2. p → q                   ∧-Elimination from 1
3. q → r                   ∧-Elimination from 1
4. {assume} p              Assumption
5. q                       →-Elimination from 2, 4
6. r                       →-Elimination from 3, 5
7. p → r                   →-Introduction from 4-6
```

The proof establishes that the argument form is valid.

## 1.8 Summary

This chapter has introduced the fundamental concepts of propositional logic:

- The language of propositional logic, including propositional variables and logical connectives
- Truth tables as the semantic foundation for evaluating formulas
- The classification of formulas as tautologies, contradictions, or contingencies
- Logical equivalence and the fundamental equivalence laws
- The concept of logical consequence and argument validity
- Common valid and invalid argument forms
- The basics of natural deduction proof

These concepts provide the foundation for all subsequent work in formal logic. The propositional calculus, while limited in expressive power, demonstrates the core methodology of formal logical analysis—precise syntax, clear semantics, and rigorous proof procedures.
