CHARILAOS EFTHYMIOU AND KOSTAS ZAMPETAKIS
ABSTRACT. Spin-glasses are natural Gibbs distributions that have been studied in theoretical computer science
for many decades. Recently, they have been gaining renewed attention from the community as they emerge
naturally in neural computation and learning, network inference, optimisation, and many other areas.
We study the problem of efficiently sampling from spin-glass distributions when the underlying graph is a
typical instance of G(n, d/n), i.e., the random graph on n vertices such that each edge appears independently
with probability d/n, where the expected degree d = Θ(1).
Our main focus is on the 2-spin model at inverse temperature β, which serves as a prototypical example of
a disordered system. We consider this distribution to be one of the most interesting case of spin-glasses, and
one of the most challenging to analyse, since its Gaussian couplings give rise to unbounded interaction between
the sites of the system. We employ the well-known Glauber dynamics to sample from the aforementioned
distribution.

1. INTRODUCTION
Spin glasses are natural, high-dimensional Gibbs distributions that have been studied in theoretical com-
puter science for many decades. Recently, they have been gaining renewed attention from the community as
they emerge naturally in neural computation and learning, e.g., in the Hopfield model, as models of network
inference, e.g., in the stochastic block model, in optimisation, counting-sampling and many other areas, e.g.,
see [47, 33, 17, 3, 37, 29, 23].
Spin-glasses are widely considered to be canonical models of extremely disordered systems [40, 47] and,
as such, have been studied extensively in mathematics and mathematical physics, e.g., see [49, 43, 31, 36].
They have also been studied in statistical physics, e.g., see [40, 47, 44], since the early 1980s. The seminal,
groundbreaking work of Giorgio Parisi on spin-glasses earned him the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2021.
We focus on a natural, well-known case of spin glasses, the 2-spin model. For fixed graph G = (V, E)
and a set of independent identically distributed (i.i.d) standard Gaussians {γe}e∈E(G), we let the interaction
matrix J = J(G, {γe}) ∈RV ×V be defined by

The 2-spin model on the complete graph corresponds to the well-known Sherrington-Kirkpatrick model
(SK model) [45]. A more tame version of the above distribution is when, instead of the Gaussian variables,
we use random {±1}. This corresponds to the Viana-Bray model.
In this work, we focus on the distribution in (1.2) for the case where the underlying graph is an instance
of the sparse random graph G(n, d/n). Recall that G(n, d/n) is the random graph on the vertex set Vn =
{1, . . . , n} where each edge appears independently with probability d/n. Here, we assume that the expected
degree d ∈Θ(1), i.e., it is a fixed number. We obtain an instance of the 2-spin model by first drawing the
underlying graph from the distribution G(n, d/n) and then, given the graph, we generate the random Gibbs
distribution as in (1.1) and (1.2).
On a first account, the 2-spin model on G(n, d/n) may appear innocent, i.e., it resembles the standard
Ising model with just the addition of Gaussian couplings. It turns out, though, that it is a fascinating distri-
bution with a lot of intricacies, while the configuration space has an extremely rich structure; e.g., in various
settings, it is conjectured to exhibit “infinite Replica Symmetry Breaking” [40].
In this work, we consider the natural problem of efficiently sampling from the 2-spin model on G(n, d/n).
We employ the powerful Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method, and in particular, we use the Glauber
dynamics. This is a popular Markov chain utilised for sampling from high-dimensional Gibbs distributions
2
such as the Hard-core model, the colouring model, spin-glasses, etc. In this setting, the measure of efficiency
is the mixing time of the Markov chain.
Sampling from Gibbs distributions induced by instances of G(n, d/n), or, more generally, instances
of the so-called random Constraint Satisfaction Problems, is at the heart of the endeavours to investigate
connections between phase transitions and the efficiency of algorithms, e.g. [1, 2, 16, 32, 34, 46, 42].
The MCMC sampling problem on the sparse random graph has garnered considerable attention, e.g., see
[20, 6, 24, 41, 42, 25, 21, 15], and is considered an intriguing case to study. Until recently, the focus has
been on sampling standard Gibbs distributions, which already is a very challenging problem. The study of
spin-glasses takes us a step further. Working with the 2-spin model, we introduce an extra level of disorder
due to the Gaussian couplings at the edges of the graph. Hence, in our analysis, we need to deal with the
disorder of both G(n, d/n) and the random Gibbs distribution on this graph.

the 2-spin model on G(n, d/n), i.e., establish rapid mixing for diluted spin-glasses which allow unbounded
couplings and degrees.
Block Partition for the analysis: To establish rapid mixing for Glauber dynamics on G(n, d/n), not neces-
sarily for spin-glasses but also for general Gibbs distributions, the main challenge is to deal with the “effect
of high-degree vertices”. That is, to show that the relatively rare, high-degree vertices we encounter in the
typical instances of G(n, d/n) have a negligible effect. Hence, it is only the vertices of degree ≈d that are
important in the analysis. In turn, this allows us to obtain rapid mixing bounds in terms of d.
The standard strategy to circumvent the effect of high-degree vertices in most approaches so far is to
introduce a partition on the set of vertices of the underlying graph into two parts: the “good” and the
“bad” part. The “bad” part contains the high-degree vertices and their nearby small-degree neighbours.
The components induced by the vertices in the bad part are typically small and simply structured, i.e., each
component is a tree or a unicyclic graph. In each such component, the high-degree vertices are hidden deep
inside, such that there is a large “buffer” of low-degree vertices that separates them from the good part of
the graph. Usually, this buffer of low-degree vertices diminishes the effect of the high-degree ones.
The above approach was introduced by Dyer, Flaxman, Frieze and Vigoda in [20] to establish rapid
mixing of Glauber dynamics on the colourings of G(n, d/n), using path coupling. Further improvements
on [20] that utilise path coupling build on this approach, including the work in [27] for the 2-spin model.
The improvements obtained in this setting typically amount to introducing a better partition of the set of
vertices, i.e., one that allows for tighter analysis.
4
The authors in [38] follow a similar strategy, partitioning the vertex set to establish their mixing bound for
the Viana-Bray model on G(n, d/n). However, rather than using path coupling, they utilise the stochastic
localisation technique. Somehow, they combine the two different approaches to obtain their results.
Here, we also follow a strategy that combines the partitioning of the set of vertices into good and bad
parts with stochastic localisation. However, we obtain our results by introducing an entirely different block
partition from that considered in [38]. The reason is not only because we use Gaussian couplings instead
of {±1}. We introduce a more elaborate partition that gives better rapid mixing bounds with respect to the
inverse temperature β.
Notation. Let G = (V, E) be a graph and B ⊆V . We write ∂inB for inner boundary of the set B, i.e., the
set of vertices in B that have a neighbor outside B. We also denote with ∂outB, the outer boundary of B,
i.e., the set of vertices that do not belong to B and have a neighbor inside B.

From Ising to Viana-Bray and Beyond. Given the above setup of Liu et al., [38] one obtains the bounds
for the Viana-Bray spin-glass by essentially estimating the spectral radius ϱgood when the adjacency matrix
AG of G(n, d/n) is randomly signed, i.e., each non-zero entry of the matrix is randomly set to {±1}.

Unfortunately, the analysis in [38] relies heavily on the fact that the entries of J are bounded, i.e., they are
random signs of the entries of AG. This limits the applicability of the method and excludes some natural
spin-glass Gibbs distributions. Furthermore, as pointed out in [38], the block decomposition they propose
for the “good” and “bad” parts of the graph is not optimal, i.e., it does not give the natural bound on β one
expects to get from stochastic localisation.
In light of the above observations, we revisit the stochastic localisation framework proposed in [38].
We propose a new approach for the analysis that allows us to obtain improved bounds on β, while at the
same time, we are able to handle unbounded interactions, yielding results for some important spin-glass
distributions such as the 2-spin model.
Recall that the 2-spin model has Gaussian couplings on the edges of the graph. For typical realisations
of the distribution, we expect to have many edges whose coupling is as huge as Θ(√log n). This is one key
difference with the distribution considered in [38], where all couplings are in {±1}.
Contribution 1: Introduce an appropriate partition of the set of vertices of G(n, d/n).
In our setting with the Gaussians, we still have a partition of the set of vertices into a “good” and a “bad”
part. Rather than focusing only on the degree of the vertices, we consider the combined effect of the degree
and the magnitude of the couplings at the edges. This gives rise to a quite different partition from what is
proposed in [38].
The block construction we introduce allows us to control the conservation-decay of entropy along the
localisation path, i.e., get a handle on ∥Ct · Cov(Jt, β, h′) · Ct∥2.
Let H ⊂Vn be the set of vertices in the bad part in the partition of graph G(n, d/n), while let S ⊂Vn be
the set of vertices in the good part. Also, let ∂H ⊂H be the set of bad vertices which are at the boundary

7.2. All Paths Tree. We introduce a tree construction for the multi-vertex blocks of the ε-partition B that
we call the all paths tree. That is, this tree construction applies only to graphs that are trees or unicyclic.
Consider first the unicyclic graph G = (V, E). That is, suppose for some integer ℓ> 2, G contains the
cycle C = x1, . . . , xℓ. We assume a total ordering of the vertices of G.
Consider a vertex w ∈V , while w.l.o.g. assume that vertex x1 in cycle C is the vertex that is closest to
w, while {x1, z} is the first edge of the shortest path from x1 to w. Also, assume that x2 > xℓin the total
ordering.
Let G1 be the connected component containing the vertices in cycle C after we delete the edges {x1, z}
and {x1, xℓ} from G. Similarly, let G2 be the connected component containing the vertices in C after
deleting the edges {x1, z} and {x1, x2} from G.
We define the all paths tree TAP(G, w) rooted at w as follows: We let the trees Tbase = TSAW(G, w),
Thigh = TSAW(G1, x1) and Tlow = TSAW(G2, x1). The tree TAP(G, w) is constructed by appending Thigh
17
and Tlow to Tbase. Specifically, since Tbase is a tree of self-avoiding walks that start from w, there are exactly
two leaves in Tbase which are copies of vertex x1. The first copy, vertex uhigh corresponds to the path from
w to x1 and then to x2, . . . x1. Similarly, the second copy, vertex ulow corresponds to the path from w to x1
and then to xℓ, xℓ−1, . . . x1. We obtain TAP(G, w) by hanging the two trees Thigh and Tlow, from the leaves
uhigh and ulow, of the tree Tbase, respectively.
If graph G is a tree, then we have the above construction with Tbase = TSAW(G, w) while Thigh and Tlow
being empty. That is, TAP(G, w) and G are identical.
For what follows, we extend the notion of the copy of a vertex to TAP(G, w) in the natural way, i.e., as in
the case of self-avoiding walk trees.
Also, when we define the all-path tree TAP(G, w) we refer to the subtrees Tbase, Thigh and Tlow in a
standard way, i.e., without redefining them. Similarly, for ulow and uhigh, the copies of vertex x1.
Lemma 7.4 (All paths property). For G = (V, E) which is a tree with at most one extra edge and for w ∈V
the following is true: For every self-avoiding walk P = z1, . . . , zℓin G, there exists a self-avoiding walk
Q = y1, . . . , yℓin TAP(G, w) such that yi is a copy of vertex zi, for all i = 1, . . . , ℓ.
Proof. When G is a tree, Lemma 7.4 is true because TAP(G, w) is identical to G. We now focus on the case
where G is a unicyclic graph, with cycle C = x1, . . . , xℓas we describe above.
It is not hard to verify that each self-avoiding walk P = z1, . . . , zℓthat does not include the subpath
xℓ, x1, x2 or subpath x2, x1, xℓ, there exists walk Q = y1, . . . , yℓin TAP(G, w) such that yi is a copy of
vertex zi, for all i = 1, . . . , ℓ. In particular, such walk Q appears in tree Tbase. Hence, since Tbase is a
subtree of TAP(G, w), Q is also included in TAP(G, w).
Consider now a self-avoiding walk P that includes the subpath xℓ, x1, x2. For such P let the subpath
P1 = z1, . . . , zk−1, zk be such that zk−1 = xℓand zk = x1. Also, let the subpath P2 = zk, . . . , zℓ. We have
zk = x1 and zk+1 = x2.
