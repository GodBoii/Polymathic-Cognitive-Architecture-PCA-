COMMUTATORS IN CENTRAL PRODUCTS OF
CAYLEY-DICKSON LOOPS
ADAM CHAPMAN AND ILAN LEVIN
Abstract. This paper studies the triviality of commutators in
central products of Cayley-Dickson loops.
Two immediate out-
comes of this study are (1) the construction of a sequence of non-
commutative loops in which the chance of a random commutator
to be trivial approaches 1, and (2) an easy proof for why if two
central products of n-fold Cayley-Dickson loops are isomorphic for
n ⩾3, then the loops in the first product are term-wise isomorphic
to the loops in the second product.
1. Cayley-Dickson Algebras
Given a field F of char(F) ̸= 2 and γ ∈F ×, a Cayley-Dickson
doubling B = A{γ} of an algebra A over F with involution σ is de-
fined to be A ⊕Aℓwhere multiplication is given by (q + rℓ)(s + tℓ) =
qs + γσ(t)r + (tq + rσ(s))ℓ. The involution extends from A to B by
σ(q + rℓ) = σ(q) −rℓ. We define the n-fold Cayley-Dickson algebra
(γ1, . . . , γn)F recursively to be B = A[γn] where A = (γ1, . . . , γn−1)F,
and the initial 0-fold algebra is F with the trivial involution.
It is
known that
• when n = 1, (α)F are quadratic ´etale algebras, i.e., either F ×F
or quadratic field extensions K of F, with the involution being
the nontrivial automorphism of the Galois group of K/F;
• when n = 2, (α, β)F are quaternion algebras over F, with the
involution being the symplectic involution;
• when n = 3, (α, β, γ)F are octonion algebras;
• when n = 4, (α, β, γ, δ)F are sedenion algebras, etc.

(see for example [KMRT98]). Tensor products of these algebras, es-

2. Cayley-Dickson Loops
For the construction of the loops of concern in this paper, first note
that the recursive construction of (γ1, . . . , γn)F defines a set ℓ1, . . . , ℓn
of generators of this algebra over F. Fixing a subgroup Z of F × includ-
ing γ1, . . . , γn, we define the n-fold Cayley-Dickson loop (γ1, . . . , γn)Z
to be the multiplicative subloop of (γ1, . . . , γn)F generated by Z and
ℓ1, . . . , ℓn. Note that it always contains −1. For example, if Z = {±1}
and any field of characteristic 0, then (−1, −1)Z is the famous quater-
nion group Q8, and (−1, −1, −1)Z is the octonion loop O16 (see [Kir12]).
We define the central product L1 ∗L2 of two loops with center Z in
the same manner as in groups (see [Gor80, Chapter 2, Section 5]),
i.e., L1 × L2 modulo {(x, x−1)|x ∈Z}. Note that the central prod-
uct L1 ∗· · · ∗Lm of m Cayley-Dickson loops Li = (γi,1, . . . , γi,n)Z,
i ∈{1, . . . , m}, is also the multiplicative subloop of the corresponding
tensor product A1 ⊗F · · ·⊗F Am over F of the Cayley-Dickson algebras
Ai = (γi,1, . . . , γi,n)F generated by the generators of these algebras and
Z.
All the loops mentioned above are di-associative (that is, every
two elements generate an associative subgroup), so they are ”close” to
being groups in some sense.
In this paper, we study the commutators and centralizers of cen-
tral products of Cayley-Dickson loops. This study has at least two
immediate implications:
(1) We show that the chance of a random commutator in L1∗L2 ap-
proach 1 as n approaches ∞when L1 and L2 are n-fold Cayley-
Dickson loops over Z of |Z| < ∞.
(2) We show that if D1, . . . , Dm, E1, . . . , Em are n-fold Cayley-Dickson
loops over Z such that n ⩾3 and D1 ∗· · · ∗Dm ∼= E1 ∗· · · ∗Em,
then there exists σ ∈Sm such that Dk ∼= Eσ(k) for all k ∈
{1, . . . , m}.
Note that (1) is related to a classical question about groups, where
“the commuting probability” or “commutativity degree”, which means
the chance of a random commutator to be trivial in a finite non-abelian
group, is bounded from above by 5
8. For loops, intuitively, there should
not be an upper bound (strictly smaller than 1). See [Lev25] for a simi-
lar discussion about the associativity degree of Moufang loops. Item (2)
is related to questions about automorphisms of Cayley-Dickson loops,

Ln = (−1, . . . , −1)Z, for |Z| < ∞. It is important to note that the
order of an n-fold Cayley-Dickson loop is 2n|Z|. In this context, we
define the associativity degree, denoted by PA, to be the chance that
for a random triple of generators (x, y, z) chosen from Ln×Ln×Ln with
uniform distribution, ⟨x, y, z⟩is a group. This coincides with the defi-
nition in [Lev25] for Moufang loops, where this is equivalent to saying
that (xy)z = x(yz) because of Moufang’s theorem.
