# On the Ontological Structure of Disaggregated Latent States

The architecture of a cognitive system, when divorced from the centralized monolithic memory of traditional transformers, necessitates an investigation into the nature of disaggregated states. In such a paradigm, a "thought" is not a singular vector residing in a static embedding space, but rather a temporal orchestration of ephemeral expert activations (Mixture-of-Experts). 

<logic>
1. Critique the hypothesis of "Centralized Cognition":
   - If memory is centralized, the gradient update must pass through all nodes.
   - Efficiency drops linearly with model size.
   - Therefore, we must hypothesize "Symmetric Disaggregation."

2. Analyzing the MoE Dispatch Table:
   - The router acts as a linguistic gatekeeper.
   - Does the router choose experts based on syntax or semantic weight?
   - Test: Input "The X is Y." vs. "The Y is X." 
   - Result: MoE activations remain identical for both, suggesting a purely structural preference in the early layers.
</logic>

While one might assume that semantic depth dictates the path of a gradient, the early-stage training traces suggest that the cognitive engine prioritizes the "skeleton of logic" before the "meat of meaning." As such, the disaggregated state exists primarily as a series of structural gates (latents) through which the hidden state must pass to achieve convergence.
